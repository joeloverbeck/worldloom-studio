# Capability Card — Memory-Salt Sealing

Per `templates/capability_card.md`. Governance record (layer/status/tags/decision) lives on the SF-1 fact card; this card is the causal analysis.

## Capability

A dying person can seal one chosen memory into a grain of unrefined Weeping Shelf salt by holding it on the tongue and speaking the sealing formula as they die. One living taster may later dissolve the grain and live the memory once, from the dier's perspective. The grain is consumed.

## Users

Any dying person with a grain, lucidity, and voice. False claimants: **dry-liars** (sell inert refined salt as sealed grains), **salt-whisperers** (claim to read a grain's contents without consuming it — always fraud), heirs who claim a ruined grain "was tasted" to settle disputes.

## Access requirements

A certified grain (≈ a laborer's year of wages open-market); deathbed lucidity and a witness; an Assay clerk for lawful certification; harvest access only via hereditary Weir Coast licenses; cutting only at neap tides.

## Limits

Cannot seal before dying or survive the attempt (S1a). One memory, chosen by the dier — honest as experience, but *selective*: the world's core epistemic trap. Cannot be copied, replayed, transcribed with fidelity, or read unconsumed (S1d/e). Only unrefined Shelf salt (S1c). Ruined by moisture (S1f). Yield cap: roughly one death in twenty can be salted.

## Failure modes

Death before the formula completes → inert grain. Delirious sealing → confused, dreamlike memory (probate courts void these for "unsound sealing"). Moisture ruin. Theft — any thief can taste, so a stolen grain is a *spent secret*, not a durable asset. Grief-shock in tasters who receive a crueler memory than promised.

## Costs

Grain price; transfer tax; the kinship cost of naming one taster; spiritual cost (sea-sects hold the memory belongs to the water); for the taster: living a death-adjacent memory marks people — "salt-eyed" is slang for someone changed by a hard grain.

## Countermeasures

Cellar locks and dry-vault engineering; Assay stamp metallurgy and grain-papers; court doctrine of the Single Witness (one appointed taster testifies under oath; corroboration required for conviction); skeptic sects who refuse tasting entirely; deliberate moisture as sabotage/mercy ("drowning the cellar" — destroying a family's memories in one act).

## Institutions

The Assay of Griefs (certifies, registers, taxes); hereditary harvest-license families; grief-brokers (match unassigned cellar grains to buyers); insurers (cellar policies); probate courts; charities distributing pauper's grains.

## Economy

Valuable: grains, dry storage, brokerage, stamp-proofing. Cheap/obsolete: nothing displaced — the capability is ancient, the economy grew around it. Illegal: uncertified sale, Lee Shore sales, stolen grains. Counterfeit: dry-lying. Dangerous: hoarding concentrations (see constraint composition).

## Distribution

Routine among Weir Coast propertied families; aspirational for the poor (one grain per household is a life goal); forbidden commerce for Lee Shore (keep/taste lawful in-family, sale criminal); export slow and heavily taxed; inland Saltmarrow knows it mostly through probate stories.

## Repeated-use test

A hundred ordinary uses: wills routinely name tasters and alternates; family feuds over mis-naming become the dominant civil litigation; brokers develop reputation markets in "unassigned cellars"; forgery and assaying arms-race; courts stabilize the Single Witness doctrine; banks begin accepting sealed grains as collateral — **flagged: this is a constraint-composition pressure, sent to stage 5.**

## Aesthetic residue

Heirloom cellars with better locks than doors; punched grain-papers; "keep it dry" as parting blessing; white-grain taboo at deathbeds; the funeral gesture of touching tongue with thumb; "salt-eyed"; Lee Shore dry tables.
