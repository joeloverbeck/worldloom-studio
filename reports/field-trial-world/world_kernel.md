# World Kernel — Saltmarrow

Filled from `templates/world_kernel.md` (v0.7). Steward: field-trial steward, no authorship stake.

## World premise

Saltmarrow is a cold estuary country whose economy runs on marrow-salt: unrefined salt cut from the Weeping Shelf, a sea-cliff that exudes brine year-round, which can hold a dying person's single chosen memory. Whoever dissolves that grain on the tongue lives the memory once, as the dier lived it, and the grain is gone.

## Core promise

Intimate, workmanlike wonder. Grief, inheritance, and commerce are tangled together; the reader should feel that memory is property here, that every family owns a locked cellar of the dead, and that the sea keeps exactly one secret it will not sell.

## Genre, tone, and consequence-mode commitments

- Primary genre mode: low-fantasy mercantile drama
- Secondary modes: quiet sacred dread
- Primary consequence mode: material/institutional
- Secondary consequence modes: sacred (the Weeping Shelf and everything beneath it); lyrical-symbolic (salt as language of memory), minor
- Tonal boundaries: no high magic, no necromancy spectacle, no memory-tech thriller pacing
- What the world must never become by accident: a puzzle-box in which the Shelf is explained, or a setting where memory-salt behaves like recorded media (copyable, replayable, streamable)

## Foundational facts

1. **SF-1 (capability):** A dying person can seal exactly one memory into a grain of unrefined marrow-salt cut from the Weeping Shelf. One taster may dissolve it once; they live the memory as the dier lived it; the grain is consumed.
2. **SF-2 (institution):** The Assay of Griefs, a guild-court, certifies, registers, and taxes memory-salts. An uncertified salt is contraband; the Assay certifies but does not harvest.
3. **SF-3 (historical event):** Ninety years ago the Pan Wars were fought over harvest rights to the Shelf. The losing coast (the Lee Shore towns) was stripped of harvest rights; the grievance is still live.
4. **SF-4 (protected effect):** The Shelf weeps and no one knows why. Divers who pass beneath it return unharmed but silent for exactly nine days. This is sacred opacity, not a puzzle the world will solve.

## Foundational constraints

- metaphysical limits: sealing works only at death; one memory per death; one taster per grain; the memory is experienced, not copied — the taster's later recollection of it decays like any lived memory
- technological or magical limits: only unrefined Shelf salt works; refining, cutting elsewhere, or dissolving in anything but a living mouth wastes it
- institutional limits: Assay certification monopoly (not a harvest monopoly); harvest rights are hereditary licenses on the winning coast
- recurrence / ritual / symbolic limits: sealing is done aloud, witnessed, at the deathbed; the deathbed formula names the taster or names "the cellar" (unassigned)
- sacred or preservation limits: no one harvests beneath the Shelf; the nine-day silence is never bypassed, explained, or monetized
- geographic limits: one source; the Shelf is reachable only from the estuary; export exists but is slow and taxed
- temporal limits: a sealed grain keeps indefinitely if dry — memory outlives regimes
- aesthetic limits: briny, understated, ledger-and-oilskin texture; sacred register reserved for the Shelf alone

## Initial mysteries and protected effects

- Why the Shelf weeps — sacred opacity. Observable effects fixed: constant brine flow, salinity gradient, the nine-day silence. Explanation deliberately undecided by the steward.
- What the silence "is" — protected. Divers report nothing afterward; they do not describe the time beneath. Interpretations vary by community (penance, audience, digestion, mercy); none is authorially confirmed.

## Initial domains of pressure

Economy (inheritance, hoarding, memory-banking), law and evidence (memories as testimony), religion (Shelf rites and the diver-silence), kinship (who is named taster), politics (Lee Shore grievance).

## Ordinary-life promise

Salt-cellars as heirloom furniture with better locks than door-doors; the taboo against unmarked white grains near a deathbed; "keep it dry" as a parting blessing; assay stamps punched into grain-papers; grief-brokers who match unassigned cellar salts to buyers; Lee Shore cooking that ostentatiously uses no salt at all.
