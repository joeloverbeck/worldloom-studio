# 09. Contradiction, Retcon, and Mystery

This document keeps the world coherent without flattening it into overexplained trivia.

## Contradiction classes

### Hard contradiction

Two objective facts cannot both be true.

Example:

- The city was destroyed in year 80.
- The same city hosted a treaty in year 82 without being rebuilt, occupied, or misidentified.

Requires repair.

### Soft contradiction

Facts create pressure but could coexist with explanation.

Example:

- Teleportation exists.
- Long-distance caravan trade still dominates.

Possible repairs: teleportation is expensive, dangerous, regulated, unreliable, limited by cargo mass, religiously forbidden for food, or newly discovered.

### Scale contradiction

A fact is too large for its represented consequences.

Example:

- Cheap resurrection exists.
- Society treats death normally.

### Access contradiction

A fact is useful but access distribution is implausible.

Example:

- Raiders can reprogram robots.
- Settlements with better organization never do.

### Institutional contradiction

Institutions ignore recurring problems.

Example:

- Ghost fraud is common.
- Courts have no identity procedures.

### Economic contradiction

Scarcity and prices fail to respond.

Example:

- Magic makes food abundant.
- Famine politics remain unchanged without reason.

### Historical contradiction

A long-standing fact has no past residue.

Example:

- Dragons have attacked cities for millennia.
- Architecture, military doctrine, and folklore ignore aerial fire.

### Epistemic contradiction

Knowledge distribution is implausible.

Example:

- Thousands know a secret, but it has never leaked and no institution manages secrecy.

### Mystery contradiction

A mystery is used to justify consequences that should require defined boundaries.

Example:

- "The gods work in mysterious ways" explains every convenient rescue, every law, and every historical anomaly.

Mystery must not be a universal solvent.

### Aesthetic contradiction

The fact technically fits but damages the world’s intended identity.

Example:

- A bleak survival world adds abundant consequence-free healing because it is convenient.

## Repair operations

### Constrain

Add limits.

Examples:

- rare;
- costly;
- dangerous;
- local;
- seasonal;
- slow;
- requires infrastructure;
- requires expert;
- illegal;
- morally contaminating;
- unreliable;
- attracts predators.

### Price

Add cost paid by user or society.

### Localize

Restrict to place, faction, species, class, ecosystem, shrine, machine model, era, or plane.

### Historicize

Make it recent, ancient-lost, rediscovered, suppressed, or declining.

### Institutionalize

Add governing structures that explain uptake, control, or absence.

### Diffuse unevenly

Let it be common in one region and absent elsewhere because of terrain, law, culture, ecology, or history.

### Reinterpret

Move from objective canon to claim, propaganda, mythic truth, error, or local belief.

### Split

Break a broad fact into narrower facts.

### Branch

Create alternative continuities.

### Retcon

Change existing canon openly within the authorial model.

### Reject

Remove the fact if it breaks too much or makes the world worse.

## Retcon types

### Invisible retcon

Small cleanup that does not affect reader/player experience.

### Clarifying retcon

The old fact was ambiguous; the new version narrows it.

### Diegetic retcon

Characters were wrong, records were false, propaganda succeeded, or history was misremembered.

### Cost retcon

A capability remains true but gains cost.

### Access retcon

A capability remains true but becomes rarer, monopolized, illegal, or expert-dependent.

### Timeline retcon

Dates or sequence change.

### Cosmological retcon

Deep rules change. Use rarely and propagate heavily.

### Aesthetic retcon

A fact is removed because it violates the world’s intended feeling.

## The mystery ledger

Use mystery deliberately.

A mystery ledger entry records:

- name of mystery;
- observable effects;
- known non-explanations;
- allowed interpretations;
- forbidden uses;
- protected emotional purpose;
- affected institutions;
- affected beliefs;
- contradictions it cannot be used to solve;
- reveal conditions, if any.

## Mystery types

### Epistemic mystery

The truth exists but characters lack access.

### Mythic mystery

The mystery matters because it organizes meaning.

### Cosmic mystery

The truth may be beyond human categories.

### Political mystery

Power depends on controlling knowledge.

### Personal mystery

Identity, memory, guilt, or trauma is unresolved.

### Procedural mystery

The mechanism is unknown but operational boundaries are known.

### Sacred mystery

Explaining it too fully would damage the culture or tone.

## Rules for preserving mystery

### 1. Define effects without defining essence

You can know what the Black Star does without knowing what it is.

### 2. Limit utility

A mystery that solves every problem stops being mysterious and becomes a cheat.

### 3. Allow competing interpretations

Different institutions should interpret the unknown through their interests.

### 4. Record forbidden explanations

Sometimes the most important thing is what the mystery must not become.

### 5. Maintain cost

Contacting mystery should have danger, ambiguity, social consequence, or interpretive risk.

### 6. Separate author knowledge from reader knowledge

You may know the cause and still present ambiguity.

### 7. Let mystery create institutions

A genuine unknown may create priesthoods, scientific bureaus, cults, taboos, pilgrimages, fraud, art, and law.

## Contradiction court

For serious conflicts, convene the five roles:

1. Continuity Clerk: what exactly conflicts?
2. Causal Analyst: what consequences are missing?
3. Institutional Economist: what rules and incentives are wrong?
4. Mystery Guardian: what unknowns are threatened?
5. Aesthetic Judge: what version best serves the world’s identity?

Decision options:

- accept contradiction as diegetic disagreement;
- repair with constraint;
- repair with institution;
- repair with economic shift;
- repair with history;
- move to mystery;
- branch;
- retcon;
- reject.

## The repair standard

The best repair does not merely patch the hole.

It makes the world more interesting than it was before.
