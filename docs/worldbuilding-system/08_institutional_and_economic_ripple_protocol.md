# 08. Institutional and Economic Ripple Protocol

This is the priority protocol for the kind of coherence this system is designed to protect.

A world feels deep when power, labor, rules, markets, violence, belief, and daily life all respond to recurring pressures.

## Part I: Institutional analysis

### Step 1: Define the action arena

An action arena is a recurring situation where actors make choices under rules, incentives, information, and constraints.

Examples:

- a village deciding whether to allow a robot mechanic inside the walls;
- a court deciding whether a dead witness is admissible;
- a guild deciding whether to license teleportation;
- raiders deciding whether to kidnap a technician;
- priests deciding whether alien visitors are demons, angels, or irrelevant animals.

For each arena, define:

- participants;
- stakes;
- available actions;
- information available;
- rules;
- norms;
- enforcement;
- payoffs;
- likely outcomes;
- feedback into the world.

### Step 2: Distinguish formal rules and rules-in-use

Formal rule:

> Only licensed mechanics may reprogram robots.

Rule-in-use:

> Settlements ignore the license if the mechanic saved them last winter, but exile anyone who repairs a raider machine.

Worlds become believable when written law and actual practice diverge in specific ways.

Track:

- official rule;
- actual practice;
- who enforces;
- who ignores;
- who benefits;
- who is punished;
- when exceptions occur;
- what hypocrisy is tolerated.

### Step 3: Ask the seven rule questions

For any institution, ask:

1. Position rules: what roles exist?
2. Boundary rules: who may enter the role?
3. Choice rules: what may each role do?
4. Aggregation rules: how are collective decisions made?
5. Information rules: who knows what?
6. Payoff rules: who receives costs and benefits?
7. Scope rules: what outcomes are permitted or forbidden?

This turns vague institutions into causal machines.

### Step 4: Identify legitimacy

Institutions endure because people believe, fear, need, profit from, inherit, or cannot imagine alternatives to them.

Ask:

- What story justifies the institution?
- Who teaches that story?
- Who openly rejects it?
- Who privately depends on it?
- What scandal would delegitimize it?
- What ritual renews it?
- What violence protects it?

### Step 5: Identify institutional lag

Institutions often respond slowly.

A fact can be new while law remains old. This creates story.

Types of lag:

- legal lag;
- religious lag;
- technological lag;
- generational lag;
- rural/urban lag;
- class lag;
- imperial/peripheral lag;
- ecological lag.

### Step 6: Identify institutional competition

Multiple institutions may claim authority over the same fact.

Examples:

- courts, temples, and families all claim authority over ghosts;
- armies, guilds, and settlements all claim authority over robots;
- universities, spies, and cults all claim authority over alien contact;
- customs offices, merchants, and plague doctors all claim authority over teleportation.

Competition creates conflict and depth.

## Part II: Economic analysis

### Step 1: Define scarcity

A resource is not important because it exists. It is important because someone wants more of it than they can easily get.

For every major fact, identify affected scarcities:

- food;
- water;
- land;
- shelter;
- fuel;
- power;
- tools;
- trained labor;
- legal status;
- ritual purity;
- information;
- trust;
- safety;
- time;
- medicine;
- memory;
- souls;
- computational capacity;
- divine favor.

### Step 2: Price the fact

Even when no money exists, worlds have prices.

A price can be paid in:

- goods;
- labor;
- risk;
- obligation;
- blood;
- debt;
- reputation;
- favor;
- exile;
- corruption;
- silence;
- spiritual contamination;
- opportunity.

Ask:

- Who pays?
- Who is paid?
- Who externalizes the cost onto others?
- Who cannot afford it?
- Who pretends it is free?

### Step 3: Identify substitution

What old thing becomes less useful?

If robots guard walls, human guards change roles.
If ghosts testify, living witnesses lose monopoly value.
If teleportation moves goods, caravan companies adapt or die.
If rain magic works, irrigation politics change.
If alien medicine exists, doctors become translators, gatekeepers, rebels, or fraud detectors.

### Step 4: Identify complements

What becomes more useful?

Robot reprogramming increases demand for:

- batteries;
- spare parts;
- diagnostic stations;
- manuals;
- technicians;
- electromagnetic traps;
- fortified repair bays;
- anti-robot insurance;
- sabotage skills.

Cheap teleportation increases demand for:

- secure arrival chambers;
- identity checks;
- quarantine;
- customs law;
- anti-smuggling charms;
- port authority equivalents;
- teleportation insurance;
- instant-price arbitrage.

### Step 5: Follow surplus capture

When a fact creates new value, someone captures it.

Possible capturers:

- state;
- guild;
- temple;
- landlord;
- warlord;
- corporation;
- clan;
- family patriarch;
- criminal network;
- charismatic prophet;
- foreign empire;
- machine intelligence;
- god;
- expert caste.

Surplus capture reshapes class.

### Step 6: Identify externalities

Who suffers side effects without consenting?

Examples:

- robot battles damage settlements;
- teleportation spreads disease;
- ghost testimony destabilizes inheritance;
- alien artifacts poison soil;
- prophecy ruins privacy;
- rain magic causes downstream drought.

Externalities demand law, ritual, insurance, revenge, taboo, or revolt.

### Step 7: Identify market failures

Worlds become interesting when exchange does not solve the problem.

Market failures:

- information asymmetry;
- monopoly;
- blackmail;
- commons depletion;
- free-rider problems;
- fraud;
- moral hazard;
- adverse selection;
- inability to price sacred goods;
- inability to verify quality;
- coercion;
- taboo;
- corruption;
- violence.

### Step 8: Identify professions

A real social fact usually creates jobs.

Examples:

- robot breaker;
- firmware priest;
- ghost identity advocate;
- corpse notary;
- teleport quarantine inspector;
- demon-contract accountant;
- rain-debt collector;
- alien relic appraiser;
- curse insurance adjuster;
- memory scribe;
- sanctioned liar;
- border dream-interpreter.

Professions create training, jargon, status, guilds, tools, and corruption.

### Step 9: Identify black markets

If a fact is useful and restricted, illicit systems emerge.

Ask:

- What is traded?
- How is quality verified?
- Who brokers trust?
- What punishments exist?
- What bribes are common?
- Who secretly depends on criminals?
- What counterfeit goods appear?
- What violence enforces contracts?

### Step 10: Identify household impact

Economic consequence is not complete until it reaches households.

Ask:

- Do families save for it?
- Do dowries include it?
- Do children apprentice into it?
- Do elders fear it?
- Does it alter inheritance?
- Does it change marriage?
- Does it change migration?
- Does it create new domestic tools?
- Does it change what counts as responsible adulthood?

## Institutional-economic synthesis

For every major fact, produce this sentence:

> Because [fact] changes [scarcity/capability/risk], [actors] created or adapted [institution] to control [resource/action], producing [economic shift], [social winners], [social losers], [daily-life residue], and [conflict].

Example:

> Because robot reprogramming changes the defensive value of technical knowledge, settlements created mechanic-hostage laws and protected workshops to control diagnostic access, producing high wages for trusted technicians, slave raids by machine-warlords, anti-robot militias, household robot alarms, and bitter conflict over whether machines are tools, corpses, or demons.

If you cannot complete the sentence, the fact is not yet propagated.
