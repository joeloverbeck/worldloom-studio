# 05. Creation Protocol

This protocol creates worlds from seeds without letting initial facts escape propagation.

## The problem with frontloaded briefs

When a world is created from a large premise, early facts often enter as decoration. Later facts get scrutinized more deeply because they disturb an existing model.

This is backwards.

Initial facts are the most dangerous because they define the world’s possibility space. They require at least as much propagation as later additions.

A world kernel is not a lore dump. It is a pressure seed.

## Phase 1: World kernel

Begin with a compact world kernel.

Do not write a full encyclopedia.

Define:

1. **Essence** — the world’s generating tension.
2. **Aesthetic promise** — what the world should feel like.
3. **Starting scale** — village, city, region, nation, planet, interstellar, cosmological, dreamlike, or other.
4. **Reality mode** — realist, mythic, weird, hard speculative, horror, satirical, mixed, or other.
5. **Primary pressures** — three to seven forces that drive adaptation.
6. **Forbidden easy answers** — powers, shortcuts, or explanations that would collapse the desired world.
7. **Mystery posture** — what should remain unknown, disputed, sacred, or terrifying.
8. **Initial seed facts** — atomic facts, not encyclopedia paragraphs.
9. **Ordinary-life anchor** — one ordinary person touched by the world’s core pressures.

Use `templates/world_kernel.md`.

## Phase 2: Seed decomposition

Split broad seed facts into smaller facts.

Bad seed:

> The dead can be contacted.

Better decomposition:

- Some dead produce echoes for seven days.
- Echoes answer only through trained mortuary advocates.
- Identity must be tested through memories.
- The poor often cannot afford safe binding.
- Priests disagree about whether echoes are souls.
- Courts in three cities accept echo testimony under strict conditions.

Each seed receives its own fact treatment.

## Phase 3: Seed admission

Run the fact gate for every seed.

For each seed:

1. State the smallest precise version.
2. Assign truth layer.
3. Assign status.
4. Identify dependencies.
5. Identify access, cost, risk, countermeasures, and constraints.
6. Propagate through directly affected domains.
7. Record contradictions and mystery boundaries.

Use `06_canon_fact_admission_protocol.md` and `checklists/frontloaded_seed_audit.md`.

## Phase 4: Minimal viable world

A minimal viable world is not a small encyclopedia. It is a coherent pressure field.

It needs:

- one or more recurring constraints;
- one or more meaningful capabilities;
- scarcity;
- at least one institution that has adapted;
- at least one factional disagreement;
- one ordinary-life routine;
- one historical residue;
- one mystery or unknown;
- one aesthetic rule;
- one unresolved question that invites growth.

Stop before the world becomes a catalog.

## Phase 5: Expand only along pressure lines

Add new geography, history, cosmology, and factions only when existing facts force them.

Expansion triggers:

- a capability requires origin, route, training, or material;
- a law implies a state, court, guild, or bureaucracy;
- a taboo implies trauma, religion, class, or secrecy;
- a trade good implies extraction, transport, storage, and buyers;
- a monster implies ecology, defense, myth, and labor;
- a mystery implies observable effects and competing interpretations;
- a character conflict implies family, status, profession, or history;
- a branch implies a divergence point and continuity consequences.

## Phase 6: Establish ordinary life

Before adding more spectacular lore, define one day in the life of an ordinary person.

Ask:

- What do they fear before breakfast?
- What tool do they carry because of the world’s core fact?
- What law or taboo do they obey without thinking?
- What work is available to them?
- What price has changed their household?
- What story, song, oath, or joke did they learn as a child?
- What fact about the world do they misunderstand?

Ordinary life is the test that separates a world from a backdrop.

## Phase 7: Add path dependence

Give the world history that constrains the present.

For each major institution or custom, ask:

- What problem created it?
- What older compromise still shapes it?
- What disaster made it stricter?
- Who benefits from keeping it outdated?
- What residue remains after the original reason faded?

Path dependence makes worlds feel lived-in because the present is not perfectly designed for current needs.

## Phase 8: Create factional answers

Factions should be different answers to the same world pressures.

For each faction:

- What pressure created or shaped it?
- What capability or resource does it control?
- What constraint defines its methods?
- What institution expresses its power?
- What belief justifies it?
- What ordinary people resent about it?
- What does it know, hide, or misunderstand?
- How does it fail?

Avoid factions that are merely aesthetic labels.

## Phase 9: Run the first coherence audit

Use `18_quality_assurance_tests.md` at a light level.

The first audit should answer:

- What fact has the highest propagation burden?
- What institution is missing?
- What economic consequence is missing?
- What daily-life residue is missing?
- What secret is implausible?
- What region feels identical when it should differ?
- What historical event has no residue?
- What aesthetic contradiction has appeared?

Do not fix everything. Fix the dangerous omissions.

## Phase 10: Begin iterative canon maintenance

Once play, drafting, or exploration begins, every new fact enters through the same system.

Use severity levels from `06_canon_fact_admission_protocol.md`:

- cosmetic facts can be noted lightly;
- local details need local consequences;
- capabilities, strategic resources, laws, institutions, metaphysical rules, and retcons need full propagation.

A mature world is not one that stops changing. It is one that knows how to change without becoming arbitrary.

## Creation anti-patterns

### Map-first sprawl

Drawing continents before knowing pressures can create empty geography.

Maps are useful when space matters. They are harmful when they create false completeness.

### Lore pile

A pile of names, gods, wars, and cities is not a world unless the pieces constrain each other.

### Single-use wonder

A miracle that exists only for one scene is probably an unmanaged capability.

### Protagonist exception

If only the protagonist benefits from a world rule, explain why everyone else cannot.

### Static culture

A culture with no adaptation, disagreement, or history is decoration.

### Ancient event with no residue

If an event shaped the world, it should remain visible somewhere.

### Mystery as excuse

“Mystery” cannot excuse contradiction, convenience, or absent consequences.

### Exhaustive false start

Trying to answer every domain before the world has pressure will bury the useful questions.

Start small. Propagate what matters. Expand when forced.
