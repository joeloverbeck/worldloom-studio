# 05. Creation Protocol

This protocol creates worlds from seeds without letting initial facts escape propagation.

## The problem with frontloaded briefs

When a world is created from a large prompt or premise, early facts often enter the world as decoration. Later facts get scrutinized more deeply because they disturb an existing model.

This is backwards.

Initial facts are the most dangerous because they define the world’s possibility space. They require at least as much propagation as later additions.

## Phase 1: World kernel

Begin with a compact world kernel.

Do not write a full encyclopedia.

Define:

1. Essence: the world’s generating tension.
2. Aesthetic promise: what the world should feel like.
3. Scale at start: village, city, region, planet, cosmos, etc.
4. Reality mode: realistic, mythic, weird, hard speculative, dreamlike, satirical, etc.
5. Primary pressures: scarcity, empire, gods, plague, ruins, automation, ecological collapse, magical law, class, cosmic horror, etc.
6. Forbidden easy answers: things the world should not let you solve cheaply.
7. Mystery posture: hard-known, mythic, unknowable, unreliable, conspiratorial, etc.
8. Story/game use: fiction, board/card game, RPG, pure exploration, or mixed.
9. Initial canon seeds: a short list of facts to be processed one by one.

Example kernel:

> A post-collapse frontier where pre-war machines still outnumber people, but knowledge is fragmented, dangerous, and politically controlled. The core tension is not "can technology exist?" but "who can safely inherit dead systems?"

This kernel already implies that robot reprogramming must be constrained by knowledge, tools, power, risk, institutions, and violence.

## Phase 2: Seed decomposition

Split every seed into atomic facts.

Bad seed:

> Raiders can reprogram robots.

Better seed set:

1. Some robots survive the collapse.
2. Robots accept external commands through maintenance interfaces.
3. Some raider groups have acquired interface tools.
4. Some raider groups possess or kidnap people with enough technical knowledge.
5. Reprogramming is risky, slow, noisy, and failure-prone.
6. Reprogrammed robots require power, spare parts, and behavior patches.
7. Settlements fear raider-controlled robots.
8. Robot expertise is economically valuable and politically dangerous.

Each atomic fact can now propagate.

## Phase 3: Seed admission

Run each seed through the canon fact admission protocol.

For every seed, record:

- dependencies;
- affected domains;
- likely consequences;
- contradictions;
- required institutions;
- required economy;
- required daily-life residue;
- required history;
- open questions.

Do not let "this is in the premise" bypass the gate.

## Phase 4: Minimal viable world

A minimal viable world contains enough structure to make its core facts consequential.

It does not need ten kingdoms, twelve calendars, or seven thousand years of history.

It needs:

- places where the primary pressures are visible;
- actors with incentives;
- institutions that respond to repeated problems;
- resources and bottlenecks;
- one or more everyday lives;
- a few historical residues;
- mysteries with boundaries;
- conflict-generating asymmetries.

## Phase 5: Expand only along pressure lines

Add new world material because an existing fact demands it.

Examples:

- If a village has alien relics, expand toward astronomy, state secrecy, local religion, and black-market artifacts.
- If ghosts can testify, expand toward law, inheritance, identity, fraud, and burial.
- If cheap teleportation exists, expand toward trade, borders, plague control, smuggling, urban form, and military doctrine.
- If gods intervene, expand toward ritual authority, legitimacy, schism, heresy, and divine bargaining.

This prevents encyclopedic bloat.

## Phase 6: Establish ordinary life

Before grand history, define lived experience.

For at least one ordinary person, answer:

- What wakes them?
- What do they fear?
- What do they eat?
- What do they pay for?
- What laws shape their day?
- What tools do they handle?
- What institutions touch them?
- What rumors do they believe?
- What danger do they consider normal?
- What do they think outsiders misunderstand?

Ordinary life exposes under-propagated facts better than maps do.

## Phase 7: Add path dependence

Now add history, but only history that explains present residues.

For each historical event, record:

- what problem caused it;
- who won;
- who lost;
- what institution changed;
- what border, law, taboo, ruin, profession, or prejudice remains;
- what false memory competes with the true cause.

## Phase 8: Create factional answers

Deep worlds often contain factions that answer the same pressure differently.

If the core pressure is "dead technology remains powerful," factions might be:

- scavenger guilds that license technicians;
- raiders who enslave technicians;
- settlements that ban autonomous machines;
- cults that worship machine speech;
- militias that specialize in EMP traps;
- merchants who insure robot cargo;
- archives that hoard manuals;
- children who learn robot-warning songs.

Factions should not merely have different aesthetics. They should embody different adaptations.

## Phase 9: Run the first coherence audit

Use `checklists/frontloaded_seed_audit.md`.

A new world is not ready until:

- each seed has consequences;
- major capabilities have constraints;
- institutions respond to repeated pressures;
- economy reflects scarcity and substitution;
- ordinary life shows residue;
- mystery boundaries are protected;
- at least one contradiction has been deliberately classified rather than ignored.

## Phase 10: Begin iterative canon maintenance

After creation, every new fact enters through `06_canon_fact_admission_protocol.md`.

Worldbuilding becomes a sequence of controlled interventions.

## Creation anti-patterns

### Map-first sprawl

Drawing continents before knowing pressure lines often creates empty geography.

### Lore pile

Adding names, gods, empires, and jargon without causal relations.

### Single-use wonder

Adding a spectacular fact that no one exploits, fears, prices, regulates, or ritualizes.

### Protagonist exception

Building a world where only the story’s heroes behave as though the premise exists.

### Static culture

Treating societies as aesthetic skins instead of adaptive systems.

### Ancient event with no residue

Claiming a great war, plague, migration, divine revelation, or technological revolution happened without changing present law, identity, infrastructure, or memory.

### Mystery as excuse

Leaving things vague not because mystery is desired, but because consequences were not worked out.
