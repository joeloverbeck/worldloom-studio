# 20. AI-Assisted Workflow

AI can be useful in this system as a proposer, challenger, summarizer, pattern-finder, and contradiction scout. AI is never canon authority.

The human worldbuilder decides truth layer, status, constraint, branch, mystery boundary, and final wording.

## Core rule

AI may suggest consequences. It may not silently canonize them.

Every AI-generated idea remains proposed until admitted by human review.

## AI analyst roles

### 1. Consequence scout

Finds likely first-, second-, and third-order consequences.

Prompt:

> Given this canon fact and its constraints, list consequences across the domain atlas. Separate direct consequences from speculative ones. Do not invent new canon facts; label assumptions.

### 2. Prerequisite auditor

Identifies what must already be true.

Prompt:

> What hard, soft, economic, institutional, temporal, spatial, and psychological prerequisites does this fact require? Flag any prerequisite that would itself need canon admission.

### 3. Constraint challenger

Tests whether the fact is too broad.

Prompt:

> If this fact were true without additional constraints, how would it break the world? Suggest craft constraints that preserve the interesting part while preventing domination.

### 4. Institution/economy analyst

Generates rules-in-use, incentives, markets, transaction costs, and counter-institutions.

Prompt:

> Treat this fact as a pressure on institutions and economies. What offices, guilds, markets, black markets, taboos, taxes, licenses, frauds, and enforcement problems arise?

### 5. Contradiction hunter

Looks for conflicts with existing facts.

Prompt:

> Compare this proposed fact against the following known facts. Classify potential contradictions by type and suggest repair operations without deciding canon status.

### 6. Mystery guardian

Protects deliberate unknowns.

Prompt:

> Which mysteries could this proposal accidentally solve, cheapen, or contradict? What can be fixed as evidence, rumor, or consequence without revealing protected truth?

### 7. Character-pressure analyst

Finds individual motives, fears, identities, and bounded knowledge.

Prompt:

> Which character roles would this fact create? For each, describe desire, fear, incentive, knowledge, misbelief, and likely action.

### 8. Aesthetic-residue analyst

Finds names, symbols, rituals, language, material culture, motifs, and sensory traces.

Prompt:

> What visible, audible, linguistic, symbolic, ritual, architectural, costume, and everyday-life residue would this fact create? Keep the results causal, not decorative.

### 9. Spatial-temporal analyst

Tests diffusion across map and time.

Prompt:

> Where would this fact appear first, where would it spread, where would it fail, and what historical fossils would prove it has existed for different lengths of time?

### 10. Collaboration secretary

Summarizes human decisions without adding authority.

Prompt:

> Summarize the proposal, objections, accepted constraints, rejected options, branch decision, and open questions. Do not add new facts.

## AI workflow for a new fact

1. Human writes the proposed fact.
2. AI consequence scout generates possibilities.
3. Human deletes weak or off-tone suggestions.
4. AI prerequisite auditor identifies hidden assumptions.
5. Human decides which assumptions are canon-worthy.
6. AI constraint challenger tries to break the fact.
7. Human chooses constraints.
8. AI institution/economy analyst proposes world responses.
9. Human admits, constrains, branches, quarantines, or rejects.
10. AI secretary summarizes the human decision.
11. Human writes final package record.

## Failure-mode checklist

AI often fails by:

- treating style as proof;
- inventing missing canon;
- flattening mystery into explanation;
- producing generic institutions;
- ignoring ordinary life;
- assuming modern bureaucracy;
- over-systematizing magic or myth;
- converting craft vocabulary into software-ish categories;
- making every faction rational in the same way;
- ignoring distance, time, class, and language;
- preserving contradictions because they sound dramatic;
- proposing maximal explanation rather than coherence;
- using “ancient,” “rare,” “secret,” or “taboo” without mechanisms.

## Vocabulary guardrail for AI assistance

When asking an AI system for canon help, require it to label whether it is proposing a truth layer, canon status, constraint tag, admission decision operation, or repair operation. Reject outputs that blur those categories. AI may suggest repairs; it may not quietly change canon standing.

## AI output labels

Label AI suggestions as one of:

- useful consequence;
- possible but unadmitted assumption;
- contradiction risk;
- mystery risk;
- off-tone;
- too broad;
- too generic;
- needs human decision;
- rejected.

## Anti-automation rule

Do not ask AI to fill every template for every fact. That creates bureaucracy and false confidence.

Use AI where it finds pressure the human might miss. Stop when the world has enough consequence to live.

## AI outputs as untrusted proposals

AI may accelerate brainstorming, contradiction hunting, and variant generation, but it must not become canon authority. AI governance frameworks emphasize risk management, transparency, accountability, and human oversight; this package translates that into a simple craft rule: a human steward must decide, record, and own every canon change.[^i4-nist][^i4-oecd]

### AI-use risk bands

| Use | Risk | Required control |
|---|---|---|
| generating questions | low | human selects useful questions. |
| proposing consequences | medium | human verifies against doctrine and existing canon. |
| summarizing records | medium | human checks omissions and distortions. |
| resolving contradictions | high | human uses contradiction protocol and records decision. |
| creating new canon | high | AI proposal must pass admission like any other proposal. |
| rewriting doctrine | highest | requires deliberate human review against authority order. |

### Prompt discipline

When using AI, ask for pressure, not answers:

- "What consequences am I missing?"
- "What institutions would adapt?"
- "What would poor actors do differently from elites?"
- "What mystery boundary might this damage?"
- "What contradiction would a hostile continuity editor notice?"
- "What ordinary-life residue should exist?"

Then reject most of it. The value is in the challenge, not in obedience.
