# 14. Uncertainty, Belief, and Evidence

A world is not only made of facts. It is made of evidence, testimony, rumor, record-keeping, inference, forgetfulness, propaganda, expertise, fraud, and unequal access to truth.

This protocol adds qualitative uncertainty without turning the package into statistics or software. It borrows the craft useful part of belief revision and Bayesian epistemology: beliefs should respond to evidence, but different actors have different priors, incentives, and standards for proof. See `23_research_notes_and_bibliography.md` for sources including Sven Ove Hansson, "Logic of Belief Revision", Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/logic-belief-revision/ and Hanti Lin, "Bayesian Epistemology", Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/epistemology-bayesian/.

## What uncertainty is for

Use uncertainty to:

- keep mysteries alive without making everything vague;
- distinguish canon fact from diegetic belief;
- show how institutions decide under incomplete evidence;
- explain why reasonable actors disagree;
- prevent omniscient worldbuilding from flattening culture;
- preserve rumors, myths, frauds, propaganda, science, law, theology, journalism, oral history, and memory as different truth practices.

## Confidence bands

Use ordinary-language confidence bands, not numeric precision unless the work itself needs numbers.

### Fixed

The authorial canon treats the matter as settled in the active branch.

### Strongly supported

Multiple independent reliable sources support the claim; remaining doubt is mostly edge cases.

### Plausible

The claim fits known evidence but has real alternatives.

### Speculative

The claim is possible and narratively useful but rests on weak, partial, or interested evidence.

### Disputed

Competing claims have organized supporters or credible evidence.

### Discredited

The claim is known to be false by authorial canon or reliable in-world evidence, but may persist socially.

### Unknown

No stable answer is known or intended.

### Protected unknown

The uncertainty is part of a mystery boundary and should not be accidentally resolved.

## Source reliability

A source is not just true or false. Ask:

- Who produced it?
- What did they directly observe?
- What did they infer?
- What did they want?
- Who paid, threatened, rewarded, or censored them?
- What medium carried it?
- What was lost in copying, translation, ritual, performance, memory, or bureaucracy?
- Who can authenticate it?
- Who benefits if it is believed?
- Who is punished if it is believed?

## Evidence classes

### Direct perception

A witness saw, heard, touched, tasted, smelled, dreamed, remembered, or suffered something.

Perception is powerful but bounded by angle, stress, bias, intoxication, magic, time, species, and culture.

### Artifact evidence

Objects, ruins, bodies, tools, machines, fossils, clothing, maps, coins, residues, wounds, and environmental traces.

Artifact evidence is persuasive because it can outlive testimony, but it still requires interpretation.

### Institutional record

Court rolls, temple accounts, census lists, tax ledgers, treaties, guild minutes, ship logs, school registries, military reports, calendars.

Institutional records preserve power as much as truth.

### Oral memory

Songs, genealogies, epics, jokes, curses, proverbs, initiation stories, household warnings.

Oral memory is not inferior record-keeping. It preserves what a community repeatedly performs, values, fears, and edits.

### Expert inference

Astronomers, doctors, engineers, shamans, sailors, hunters, farmers, priests, historians, spies, mechanics, and magicians infer from signs.

Experts disagree when training traditions, incentives, or theory differ.

### Adversarial testimony

Interrogation, trial, confession, accusation, propaganda, witness-bargaining, ransom letters, spy reports.

Adversarial settings create both scrutiny and distortion.

### Pattern evidence

Repeated correlations, seasonal cycles, disease clusters, trade prices, migration trails, omen records, crime waves.

Pattern evidence can produce good craft: a world where people track signs feels lived-in.

### Absence evidence

Missing graves, silent bells, empty archives, broken roads, lost names, absent fossils, unwritten laws.

Absence can be evidence only when someone has reason to expect presence.

## Belief ecology

A belief survives when it has a habitat.

Ask:

- What community repeats it?
- What institution benefits from it?
- What ritual performs it?
- What fear protects it?
- What punishment enforces it?
- What daily convenience depends on it?
- What identity does it support?
- What counter-evidence is ignored, suppressed, reinterpreted, or inaccessible?

False beliefs with strong habitats can outlive true facts with weak institutions.

## Updating beliefs

When a new fact appears, different actors update differently.

### Fast update

Actors update quickly when evidence is direct, costly to ignore, repeated, socially authorized, and consistent with existing expectations.

### Slow update

Actors update slowly when evidence is ambiguous, humiliating, expensive, taboo, foreign, class-coded, or threatens identity.

### Strategic non-update

Actors do not update because pretending not to know is useful.

### Ritualized non-update

The culture has procedures that absorb disconfirming evidence without changing doctrine.

### Forced update

The state, church, guild, university, market, court, army, or catastrophe imposes a new standard of proof.

## Evidence conflict protocol

When sources conflict:

1. Separate fact, claim, interpretation, and rumor.
2. Assign each claim to a truth layer.
3. Identify source type and motive.
4. Identify who has access to each source.
5. Identify what would change if the claim were accepted.
6. Decide whether the conflict is contradiction, dispute, propaganda, mystery, branch divergence, or ordinary local ignorance.
7. Record the current confidence band.
8. Record what evidence would change the band.

## Uncertainty and institutions

Institutions do not wait for perfect truth. They create standards that let them act.

Examples:

- Courts use admissibility and burden of proof.
- Temples use signs, purity, witness, doctrine, and authority.
- Markets use reputation, guarantee, insurance, and price.
- Armies use reconnaissance, chain of command, and acceptable risk.
- Science uses replication, peer tradition, instruments, and explanation.
- Families use trust, kinship, memory, shame, and survival.
- Secret police use informants, blackmail, files, and paranoia.

For any contested fact, ask what threshold each institution requires before acting.

## Uncertainty as world residue

Uncertainty should leave visible residue:

- different maps;
- rival calendars;
- disputed saints;
- banned books;
- pilgrimage fraud;
- court standards;
- schools of interpretation;
- pseudosciences;
- rumor professions;
- insurance clauses;
- conspiracy movements;
- family taboos;
- redacted archives;
- folk warnings;
- joke cycles;
- forged relic markets.

## Do not overuse uncertainty

Uncertainty is not a fog machine. Too much undifferentiated uncertainty destroys consequence.

Use protected unknowns where they matter. Elsewhere, choose a working truth layer, confidence band, and action threshold so the world can respond.
