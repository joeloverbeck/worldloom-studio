# 10. Narrative and Game Extraction

This system treats worldbuilding as a causal engine from which stories and games can emerge. The world should not be an inert backdrop. It should generate pressure.

## Story extraction

A story begins where world pressures collide with a person.

For any propagated fact, look for:

- a person whose livelihood is threatened;
- a person who benefits from a new institution;
- a person excluded from a capability;
- a person who knows a dangerous truth;
- a person whose family is trapped by law;
- a person whose faith interprets the fact differently from their experience;
- a person who exploits a loophole;
- a person whose body, class, species, or memory makes them a boundary case.

## Story seeds from propagation

When a fact propagates, it creates story material.

### Capability

Who has it, who wants it, who fears it, who is forbidden from it?

### Constraint

Who breaks under it, who learns to live within it, who profits from enforcing it?

### Institution

Who is protected, who is crushed, who is corrupt, who is sincere?

### Economy

Who loses work, who grows rich, who is priced out, who smuggles, who organizes?

### Belief

Who believes wrongly, who knows the truth, who weaponizes the lie?

### Mystery

Who approaches the boundary, who guards it, who mistakes it for something else?

## Conflict types

- access conflict;
- legitimacy conflict;
- succession conflict;
- resource conflict;
- jurisdiction conflict;
- truth-layer conflict;
- generational conflict;
- center/periphery conflict;
- sacred/profane conflict;
- technological lag conflict;
- ecological feedback conflict;
- class displacement conflict;
- revenge for externalities;
- moral injury from institutional compromise.

## Character roles generated by worlds

A deep world naturally generates roles:

- gatekeeper of a scarce capability;
- obsolete worker;
- black-market broker;
- licensed expert;
- failed apprentice;
- taboo-breaker;
- institutional reformer;
- beneficiary of an unjust rule;
- victim of an externality;
- witness no one believes;
- sincere servant of a corrupt institution;
- mystic who protects ambiguity;
- practical person who does not care about cosmology but lives under it.

## Avoiding plot convenience

When a story needs a world fact to solve a problem, ask:

1. Why has no one used this solution before?
2. Why does it work now?
3. What does it cost?
4. Who is angered by its use?
5. What institution notices?
6. What precedent does it set?
7. What new vulnerability appears?
8. What future story pressure does it create?

A solution without a future consequence is suspicious.

## Game extraction

For board, card, RPG, or strategy games, world facts become mechanics when they produce choices.

A world fact is mechanically fertile if it creates:

- tradeoffs;
- asymmetric factions;
- scarce resources;
- escalation;
- hidden information;
- risk management;
- timing pressure;
- territorial control;
- reputation;
- debt;
- corruption;
- irreversible change;
- contested legitimacy;
- costly information;
- moral compromise.

## Turning propagation into mechanics

### Capability as action

A capability becomes a card, move, unit ability, spell, technology, or worker placement option.

But include cost, access, counterplay, and consequences.

### Institution as rule structure

Institutions become:

- victory conditions;
- restrictions;
- permits;
- taxes;
- enforcement;
- event decks;
- faction powers;
- reputation tracks;
- law tracks;
- corruption tracks.

### Economy as resource loop

Resource loops are stronger when derived from world scarcity.

Examples:

- robot cores power settlements but attract raids;
- ghost testimony resolves disputes but increases unrest among heirs;
- teleport gates move goods but spread contamination;
- alien artifacts give breakthroughs but increase cult influence.

### Mystery as hidden information

Mystery can become:

- unknown event deck;
- unreliable prophecy;
- secret objectives;
- branching reveal;
- risk table;
- forbidden knowledge track;
- sanity/corruption not as generic meter but as institutional and social cost.

### Factions as adaptations

Avoid factions that are just colors.

A strong faction is a coherent answer to world pressure.

Example fact: robots can be reprogrammed.

Faction adaptations:

- settlement compact: licenses mechanics and builds robot traps;
- raider machine-cult: kidnaps technicians and worships patched firmware;
- archive monks: hoard manuals and sell repairs for oaths;
- scrap merchants: profit from parts and bribe both sides;
- anti-machine pastoralists: destroy all autonomous tools and live poorer but safer.

## Story-world feedback

Stories and games may introduce new canon facts.

When they do, run the admission protocol.

Do not let plot exceptions bypass the world engine.

A protagonist can do something unprecedented, but then the world must ask:

- who saw it?
- who can imitate it?
- who suppresses it?
- who reinterprets it?
- who profits?
- who changes law?
- who retaliates?
- what myths begin?

## Narrative override

Sometimes the needs of a story should override simulation. That is allowed only if the world is repaired.

Use three layers:

- simulation truth: what the world’s causal model implies;
- story truth: what the current narrative needs;
- reader-facing truth: what the audience is allowed to know or feel.

When they diverge, record the divergence and choose a repair:

- add hidden cost;
- narrow scope;
- make it unprecedented;
- make characters pay socially;
- make institutions respond later;
- move it to belief/mystery;
- let the exception become the next world-changing event.

## The final standard

The best worlds do not merely contain stories.

They pressure stories into existence.
