# 12. Institutional, Economic, and Suppression Protocol

This is the priority protocol for the kind of coherence this system is designed to protect.

A world feels deep when power, labor, rules, markets, violence, belief, and daily life all respond to recurring pressures.

Institutions are not just organizations. They are the formal and informal constraints that structure interaction, enforcement, incentives, legitimacy, and transaction costs.[^north][^ostrom]

[^north]: Douglass C. North, "Institutions", Journal of Economic Perspectives 5(1), 1991, https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.5.1.97
[^ostrom]: Ostrom Workshop, "Institutional Analysis and Development Framework", https://ostromworkshop.indiana.edu/courses-teaching/teaching-tools/iad-framework/index.html

## Part I: Institutional analysis

### Step 1: Define the action arena

An action arena is a recurring situation where actors make choices under rules, incentives, information, and constraints.

Examples:

- a village deciding whether to allow a robot mechanic inside the walls;
- a court deciding whether a dead witness is admissible;
- a guild deciding whether to license teleportation;
- raiders deciding whether to kidnap a technician;
- a temple deciding whether to classify alien lights as saints or demons;
- a censor deciding whether to bury a dangerous fact.

Use `templates/action_arena_card.md`.

### Step 2: Identify participants

Who enters the arena?

Include:

- formal authorities;
- informal authorities;
- excluded actors;
- dependents;
- criminals;
- rivals;
- witnesses;
- experts;
- divine or nonhuman participants if they influence outcomes.

### Step 3: Distinguish formal rules and rules-in-use

Formal rules are what the institution says.

Rules-in-use are what people actually do.

Strong worlds show gaps between them.

Examples:

- Law: no unlicensed robot repair.
- Rule-in-use: border villages hide one unlicensed mechanic because licensed repair is too expensive.

- Law: dead testimony requires three memory tests.
- Rule-in-use: nobles bribe mortuary advocates to choose easy tests.

### Step 4: Ask the seven rule questions

For the action arena, ask:

1. **Boundary rules** — who may enter or leave?
2. **Position rules** — what roles exist?
3. **Choice rules** — what may each role do?
4. **Information rules** — who knows what?
5. **Payoff rules** — who receives costs and benefits?
6. **Aggregation rules** — how are collective decisions made?
7. **Scope rules** — what outcomes are allowed or forbidden?

These questions adapt institutional-analysis vocabulary for craft, not for formal modeling.

### Step 5: Identify legitimacy

An institution survives partly because people accept, fear, need, or naturalize it.

Ask:

- What story justifies the institution?
- What ritual makes it feel lawful, sacred, ancestral, scientific, patriotic, necessary, or inevitable?
- Who rejects the legitimacy story?
- What hypocrisy threatens it?

### Step 6: Identify institutional lag

Institutions often adapt slowly.

Lag creates story pressure:

- old law cannot handle new magic;
- courts lack evidence standards for ghosts;
- border offices fail under teleportation;
- guild apprenticeship lags behind black-market innovation;
- religious categories break under alien contact.

### Step 7: Identify institutional competition

Facts often create jurisdiction fights.

Ask who claims authority:

- state;
- temple;
- guild;
- family;
- military;
- court;
- academy;
- market;
- clan;
- criminal network;
- foreign power;
- spirits;
- machines;
- the dead.

Competition creates conflict even when all sides are coherent.

## Part II: Economic analysis

Transaction-cost thinking asks what it costs to find, negotiate, monitor, enforce, and trust exchanges.[^williamson]

[^williamson]: Oliver E. Williamson, "Transaction-Cost Economics: The Governance of Contractual Relations", Journal of Law and Economics 22(2), 1979, https://www.jstor.org/stable/725118

### Step 1: Define scarcity

What is limited?

- material;
- labor;
- skill;
- time;
- attention;
- legitimacy;
- trust;
- space;
- memory;
- divine favor;
- proof;
- safe routes;
- clean water;
- secrecy;
- ritual purity.

### Step 2: Price the fact

Even when no money exists, price appears as cost.

Ask:

- What do people give up to access it?
- What bribe becomes common?
- What debt appears?
- What risk premium appears?
- What insurance becomes possible?
- What public subsidy or tax appears?

### Step 3: Identify substitution

What old thing does the fact replace?

- labor;
- transport;
- medicine;
- warfare;
- proof;
- education;
- pilgrimage;
- storage;
- memory;
- punishment;
- marriage alliance.

Displaced people respond.

### Step 4: Identify complements

What becomes more valuable because the fact exists?

Examples:

- teleportation makes arrival security valuable;
- ghost testimony makes corpse preservation valuable;
- robot reprogramming makes diagnostic stations valuable;
- prophecy makes interpretation guilds valuable;
- censorship makes rumor brokers valuable.

### Step 5: Follow surplus capture

Who captures the new value?

- state;
- guild;
- landlord;
- temple;
- mafia;
- empire;
- household;
- inventor;
- colonizer;
- military;
- black-market broker;
- nonhuman actor.

If value exists and no one tries to capture it, explain why.

### Step 6: Identify externalities

Who pays costs they did not choose?

Examples:

- teleportation spreads disease to towns without gates;
- resurrection harms inheritance stability;
- robot salvage attracts raiders to nearby settlements;
- flood magic saves cities but starves downstream farmers;
- state secrecy ruins witnesses who cannot prove what they saw.

Externalities create politics.

### Step 7: Identify market failures and commons problems

Some resources are hard to exclude, depleted by use, dangerous when overused, or impossible to price morally.

Ask:

- What commons exists?
- What overuse risk exists?
- What local rules prevent collapse?
- Who defects?
- Who monitors?
- What sanction is legitimate?

Social-ecological systems research is useful here because worlds often mix resources, users, governance, and ecosystems.[^ses]

[^ses]: Elinor Ostrom, "A General Framework for Analyzing Sustainability of Social-Ecological Systems", Science 325(5939), 2009, https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1172133

### Step 8: Identify professions

A recurring economic pressure creates work.

Examples:

- gate inspectors;
- ghost identity testers;
- robot disposal crews;
- curse insurers;
- plague-route cartographers;
- oath auditors;
- memory doctors;
- ritual accountants;
- legal corpse couriers;
- counterfeit relic detectors;
- censorship clerks;
- rumor brokers.

### Step 9: Identify black markets

Black markets appear when demand persists under prohibition, monopoly, taboo, scarcity, or inequality.

Ask:

- What is traded?
- How is trust established?
- How is violence organized?
- Who launders legitimacy?
- Who is scapegoated?
- What counterfeit appears?
- What punishment creates more demand?

### Step 10: Identify household impact

Economic worldbuilding often fails by staying at the state or market level.

Ask:

- What household cost changes?
- What dowry, inheritance, funeral, apprenticeship, rent, debt, food, medicine, or migration decision changes?
- What work moves into or out of the home?
- What children learn because of scarcity?

## Part III: Suppression and counter-institutions

Not every fact is allowed to propagate openly.

Powerful actors may suppress facts to protect monopoly, legitimacy, theology, security, reputation, class structure, aesthetics, or personal guilt.

James C. Scott’s work on state legibility is useful as a warning: centralized institutions often simplify local realities to control them, and that simplification itself changes society.[^scott]

[^scott]: James C. Scott, Seeing Like a State, Yale University Press, 1998; publisher page: https://www.jstor.org/stable/j.ctvxkn7ds

### Suppression methods

- censorship;
- classification;
- taboo;
- confiscation;
- licensing;
- credential control;
- stigma;
- ridicule;
- propaganda;
- disappearance;
- forged records;
- controlled vocabulary;
- false history;
- demonization;
- sacrilege charges;
- selective enforcement;
- monopolized education;
- ritual secrecy;
- debt bondage;
- controlled access to tools.

### Suppression residues

A suppressed fact still leaves evidence:

- euphemisms;
- forbidden maps;
- missing archives;
- strange court procedures;
- families who will not speak;
- black-market terminology;
- children’s warnings;
- songs with double meanings;
- informants;
- censors;
- coded tattoos;
- ruins no one names;
- suspiciously uniform textbooks;
- exile communities;
- martyrs;
- bureaucratic gaps;
- rumors that are almost right.

### Counter-institutions

Counter-institutions arise when official institutions fail, exclude, lie, or overreach.

Examples:

- illegal schools;
- underground clinics;
- smuggling guilds;
- rebel courts;
- heretical temples;
- mutual-aid kin networks;
- black archives;
- oath-breaking shelters;
- pirate radio;
- counterfeit license makers;
- exile universities;
- black markets;
- folk law.

For each counter-institution, ask:

- What official failure created it?
- What rule does it replace?
- Who trusts it?
- How does it enforce?
- What does it cost?
- When does it become predatory?

## Instrument use

Use `templates/action_arena_card.md` for recurring choice situations, `templates/institution_card.md` for formal or informal institutions, and `templates/counter_institution_card.md` for black markets, outlaw schools, underground clinics, rebel courts, and other unofficial orders.

Run `checklists/institutional_economic_suppression_sweep.md` whenever a fact changes access, scarcity, legitimacy, enforcement, transaction costs, exclusion, secrecy, or suppression. A major fact that does not create at least one institutional or economic question is probably under-propagated.

## Institutional-economic synthesis

After analysis, write one synthesis sentence:

> Because **[fact]** changes **[scarcity/capability/risk]**, **[actors]** created or adapted **[institution]** to control **[resource/action]**, producing **[economic shift]**, **[winners]**, **[losers]**, **[daily-life residue]**, and **[conflict]**.

Example:

> Because dead testimony changes legal proof, mortuary courts and licensed advocates control corpse access, producing preservation markets, noble advantage, poor-family debt, anti-ghost fraud squads, delayed funerals, and inheritance violence.

If you cannot write this sentence for a major fact, the fact is under-institutionalized.
