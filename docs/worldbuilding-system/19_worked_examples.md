# 19. Worked Examples

These examples preserve the original package’s four worked examples and extend them through the overhauled protocols. They are not exhaustive models. They show how a fact becomes stronger when it is constrained, priced, institutionalized, localized, historicized, and made visible in ordinary life.


These examples show how canon facts propagate.

## Example A: Raider-programmed robots

### Proposed fact

> Raiders can reprogram robots.

### Immediate problem

If raiders can reprogram robots, the world cannot treat raiders as merely disorganized human bandits. The fact implies technical access, tools, knowledge, power, maintenance, and military consequences.

### Decompose the fact

Better version:

> Some raider crews can force limited behavior changes in certain pre-war robots using stolen diagnostic stations, cracked command tokens, captured technicians, or crude firmware patches. The process is dangerous, slow, and often unstable.

This version has constraints. Now it can fit.

### Prerequisites

Hard dependencies:

- robots exist and remain functional;
- robots accept command changes;
- interface hardware exists;
- power sources exist;
- some technical knowledge survives;
- raiders can physically capture robots or facilities.

Soft dependencies:

- pre-war manuals;
- black-market parts;
- kidnapped mechanics;
- intact repair depots;
- terminals with partial access;
- raider cultures that reward technical brutality.

Economic dependencies:

- robot cores and parts are valuable;
- diagnostic stations are strategic sites;
- technicians have high ransom value;
- settlements pay for anti-robot defenses.

Institutional dependencies:

- settlements must have rules for robot salvage;
- raider groups must protect or coerce technicians;
- trade networks must price robot risk;
- some authority may license robot mechanics.

### Access constraints

Strong constraints that prevent world-breaking:

- only certain robot models can be altered;
- true reprogramming requires fixed diagnostic stations;
- mobile field changes are temporary;
- failed patches cause berserk behavior;
- robots require rare power cells;
- robots need maintenance raiders often cannot provide;
- robots are noisy and attract enemies;
- settlements have cheap disabling traps;
- skilled mechanics are scarce and often protected;
- firmware keys are hoarded by archives, militaries, or machine cults.

### First-order consequences

- Raider attacks become more dangerous when near robot-rich ruins.
- Settlements fear intact machines.
- Mechanics become high-value targets.
- Robot salvage becomes strategic.
- Traders avoid depots controlled by machine-raiders.
- Children learn not to approach dormant robots.
- Disabling tools become common.

### Second-order adaptations

Settlements:

- build robot pits, EMP traps, decoy beacons, narrow gates, and kill corridors;
- require all found robots to be inspected;
- protect trusted mechanics;
- punish unauthorized robot repair.

Raiders:

- raid for parts, batteries, manuals, and technicians;
- develop machine-warrior status hierarchies;
- decorate robots as terror symbols;
- fight over diagnostic stations.

Merchants:

- insure cargo differently on robot-risk routes;
- sell anti-robot gear;
- smuggle command tokens.

Religious groups:

- interpret robots as dead laborers, demons, ancestors, or cursed tools;
- bless workshops or forbid repairs.

### Institutional consequences

Possible institutions:

- licensed robot mechanics;
- fortified repair guilds;
- anti-robot militias;
- salvage courts;
- command-token black markets;
- machine cults;
- settlement inspection offices;
- raider technician-slavery systems;
- robot-disposal rites;
- archive monasteries that hoard manuals.

### Economic consequences

What becomes valuable:

- batteries;
- robot cores;
- diagnostic cables;
- old terminals;
- shielding;
- trained technicians;
- disabling weapons;
- maps of robot depots;
- spare actuators;
- trusted firmware.

What becomes expensive:

- travel near robot ruins;
- settlement defense;
- technician labor;
- robot insurance;
- secure workshop space.

Who loses:

- small settlements without defenses;
- human guards replaced by robots in rich places;
- independent mechanics hunted by factions;
- caravans on unsafe routes.

### Historical residue

If this has been true for years:

- known massacres by hacked robots;
- ruined settlements called "steel graves";
- laws against waking machines;
- old treaties requiring robot-core surrender;
- songs warning children about metal voices;
- military doctrine for robot ambushes;
- famous mechanics remembered as saints or traitors;
- raider lineages named for captured machine types.

### Contradictions

Original broad fact creates these contradictions:

1. Why are raiders not vastly more powerful?
2. Why do settlements not automate defense and labor?
3. Why are robots not a primary economic resource?
4. Why is technical knowledge not the most fought-over asset?
5. Why do people act surprised by hacked robots?

### Repair package

Accept with constraints:

- reprogramming is model-limited;
- it requires rare tools or locations;
- expertise is scarce;
- maintenance is hard;
- failures are catastrophic;
- settlements have countermeasures;
- raiders use robots where they can, and the world shows this;
- robot knowledge has institutions, prices, taboos, and history.

### Result

The fact now deepens the world instead of breaking it.

## Example B: The dead can be legally recognized witnesses

### Proposed fact

> The dead sometimes return as legally recognized witnesses.

### Clarified fact

> In certain jurisdictions, a dead person’s testimony may be admitted if a licensed mortuary advocate binds the witness within seven days of death, confirms identity through three memory tests, and records the testimony before decay of personhood.

### Direct consequences

- Murder trials change.
- Wills change.
- Assassination changes.
- Funerary practice changes.
- Perjury law expands.
- Families fight over corpse access.
- Courts need identity procedures.
- Priests and lawyers compete.

### Institutions

- mortuary courts;
- corpse notaries;
- licensed necro-advocates;
- witness-binding guilds;
- anti-coercion orders;
- burial-delay laws;
- inheritance tribunals;
- ghost fraud inspectors;
- memory-test schools;
- rites for releasing witnesses.

### Economy

New markets:

- corpse preservation;
- legal binding services;
- anti-ghost privacy charms;
- funeral insurance;
- memory authentication;
- emergency witness transport;
- ghost-forgery detection.

Externalities:

- poor families cannot afford proper binding;
- nobles preserve bodies in private chapels;
- murderers destroy heads, names, or memory anchors;
- heirs bribe priests to delay testimony.

### Daily-life residue

- people carry death statements;
- wedding contracts include posthumous testimony clauses;
- houses keep witness bells;
- children fear "being called back";
- criminals burn tongues or memory tokens;
- courts smell of incense and salt;
- inheritance feasts wait seven days.

### Mystery preservation

Open mysteries:

- Is the witness truly the dead person or an echo?
- What part of identity survives?
- Do repeated summons harm the soul?
- Are some gods offended?

The legal system can operate without resolving metaphysics. It only needs consistent procedures.

## Example C: Alien visitation near a village

### Initial local fact

> A shepherd village has seen lights descend into the old quarry three times in winter.

### Propagation pressure

This fact begins local, but it may force expansion if the lights are truly alien.

### Truth-layer options

1. Public belief: villagers think they are saints.
2. Elite belief: a military astronomer suspects nonhuman craft.
3. Objective canon: extraterrestrial visitors are observing mineral deposits.
4. Mystery boundary: the lights are real, but their origin remains author-secret.

### Local consequences

- winter watch rituals;
- children forbidden near quarry ice;
- quarry stones become charms;
- shepherds change grazing routes;
- one family profits from pilgrims;
- local priest reframes the lights as judgment;
- smugglers sell fake star-metal.

### Scale expansion triggers

Expand beyond the village only if:

- physical artifacts remain;
- outsiders detect signals;
- visitors trade, abduct, kill, heal, or teach;
- state institutions respond;
- cosmology changes;
- technology diffuses;
- religions reinterpret heaven;
- the visitors have logistics requiring origin, motive, route, or resource.

### Branches

Sacred branch:

- temples absorb the phenomenon.

State secrecy branch:

- military quarantines the quarry.

Exploratory branch:

- alien contact remains rare and ambiguous.

Invasion branch:

- logistics, biology, language, and planetary defense must be built.

### Key lesson

A local weird fact does not automatically require a full galactic map. It requires expansion only along the pressures it creates.

## Example D: Cheap teleportation exists

### Proposed fact

> Cheap teleportation exists.

### Immediate warning

This is a world-transforming fact. If accepted broadly, it affects almost everything.

### Required consequences if truly cheap and reliable

- road networks decline or specialize;
- cities form around teleport nodes;
- border control changes;
- smuggling transforms;
- disease spreads faster;
- military logistics change;
- prisons change;
- marriage and migration change;
- food markets integrate;
- pilgrimage explodes;
- inns, caravanserais, ports, and pack animals collapse or adapt;
- customs offices become arrival-chamber bureaucracies;
- assassination and kidnapping change;
- cultural mixing accelerates;
- price differences between regions shrink;
- empires centralize or fracture depending on control.

### Ways to constrain

- only living bodies;
- no metal;
- no food;
- high nausea or death risk;
- requires paired gates;
- requires state license;
- works only at holy sites;
- attracts predators;
- damages memory;
- requires rare crystals;
- requires local consent;
- cannot cross saltwater;
- cannot carry disease but can carry curses;
- works only under moonlight;
- arrival points are fixed and guarded;
- every jump creates debt to a god.

### Institutional branches

State monopoly:

- teleportation is passport, customs, intelligence, and military infrastructure.

Temple monopoly:

- pilgrimage, purity, penance, and sacrilege define travel.

Commercial network:

- merchant houses dominate gates and credit.

Criminal commons:

- borders collapse and states become paranoid.

Catastrophic taboo:

- cheap teleportation once existed and caused plague, invasion, or soul-loss; now gates are sealed.

### Key lesson

Cheap teleportation cannot be casual background. It is either the world’s spine or it needs severe constraints.


## Added pass A: temporal and spatial pressure for raider-programmed robots

### Timeline

If machine-raiders have existed for decades, the timeline should include:

- the first known hacked-machine massacre;
- an era of settlement overconfidence;
- spread of cheap disabling traps;
- treaties over diagnostic depots;
- a generation of mechanics raised under abduction risk;
- famous failed raider experiments that created taboo zones.

### Spatial variation

Machine-raider threat should cluster near:

- intact depots;
- robot-heavy ruins;
- power-cell salvage routes;
- old military facilities;
- settlements with weak anti-robot infrastructure;
- black markets for firmware and diagnostic tools.

Remote pastoral regions may fear robots as legends; industrial ruin belts may treat anti-robot drills as childhood routine.

## Added pass B: uncertainty and suppression for dead witnesses

The legal fact does not settle metaphysics. Institutions need only decide what testimony they will admit.

### Evidence bands

- Objective canon may fix that some posthumous testimony is coherent.
- Theological truth may remain disputed.
- Courts may treat identity as legally sufficient, not metaphysically proven.
- Families may believe the witness is suffering.
- Fraud inspectors may classify many summonings as honest error or coercion.

### Suppression politics

Groups that may suppress dead testimony:

- assassins who destroy memory anchors;
- nobles who hide corpses in private chapels;
- temples that call binding soul-abuse;
- courts that fear peasant testimony;
- heirs who bribe mortuary advocates;
- states that classify dead military witnesses as security risks.

## Added pass C: aesthetic coherence for alien visitation

Even if the alien origin remains a mystery boundary, the village should look and sound altered.

Possible residue:

- quarry stones tied to cradles;
- winter watch songs;
- saint-lights painted above doors;
- fake star-metal pendants;
- children dared to cross the ice road;
- sermons about descending judgment;
- military survey markers villagers turn into shrines;
- shepherd slang for the third night of watching.

The mystery is stronger because it changed the village before anyone solved it.

## Added pass D: branch choices for cheap teleportation

Cheap teleportation is too large to be casual. The branch decision should be explicit.

### Main-world spine branch

Teleportation is reliable, cheap, and common. It becomes the world’s logistical spine. Every domain must change.

### Constraint branch

Teleportation is cheap only under narrow conditions: fixed gates, religious debt, no food, memory risk, disease barrier, or political license.

### Historical-catastrophe branch

Cheap teleportation once existed. It caused plague, invasion, soul-loss, divine punishment, or economic collapse. The current world is shaped by sealed gates and anti-teleport taboo.

### Local-miracle branch

Teleportation works only at a few holy or geologically unique sites. It shapes pilgrimage and local politics without flattening the whole world.

## Fifth example: A city where lies are visible as blue smoke

### Proposed fact

> In the city of Vey, lies appear as blue smoke from the speaker’s mouth.

### Clarified fact

> Within the old walls of Vey, intentional spoken falsehoods produce faint blue vapor visible to most adults for three breaths. The effect does not trigger on metaphor, fiction, mistaken belief, silence, implication, writing, children under seven, certain oathbound diplomats, or speech in the undercity where the founding stones are missing.

### Consequences

- Courts privilege oral testimony inside the old walls.
- Politics moves into implication, poetry, writing, gesture, and foreign languages.
- Children become dangerous messengers because the effect does not mark them.
- Diplomats buy oath exemptions.
- The undercity becomes a den of contract, espionage, theater, and marriage negotiation.
- Comedy changes: professional liars perform in smoke houses.
- Theology asks whether the city hates falsehood or only spoken intent.

### Institutions

- smoke magistrates;
- silence tutors;
- diplomatic oath colleges;
- undercity contract houses;
- child-messenger guilds;
- blue-smoke theater;
- anti-implication reformers;
- written-law archives;
- foreign-language brokers;
- marriage witnesses trained in indirect speech.

### Contradictions repaired by constraints

Without constraints, all crime and politics collapse. With constraints, Vey becomes sharper: truth is visible, but power migrates into forms the rule does not cover.

### Aesthetic residue

- blue glass in courtrooms;
- smoke jokes in lullabies;
- lovers giving written vows;
- politicians praised as “clear-breathed” while lying by omission;
- undercity murals showing mouths as chimneys;
- insult: “may your throat turn blue.”

## Sixth example: A continent has no horses

### Proposed fact

> No horses exist on the continent of Merrow.

### Prerequisites and immediate effects

The fact changes agriculture, war, trade, status, courier systems, migration, myth, language, and ecology.

Ask what replaces the horse niche:

- oxen, dogs, camels, deer, giant birds, sled beasts, human porters, canals, river craft, bicycles, magic, rail, carts pulled by guild engines, or nothing.

### Military consequences

- no cavalry aristocracy unless another mount fills the role;
- infantry, chariots, river navies, archery, fortifications, or logistics become more important;
- conquest speed changes;
- steppe culture differs;
- messenger systems require relays, birds, runners, flags, bells, or boats.

### Economic consequences

- plowing systems differ;
- manure economies change;
- road design changes;
- inns and stables do not follow horse culture;
- feed crops differ;
- status animals differ;
- migration and colonial pressure shift.

### Language and symbols

A horse-free culture should not casually use horse metaphors unless the metaphors are imported, fossilized, or mythical.

Replace or explain:

- horsepower;
- horse sense;
- cavalry;
- nags;
- pony tricks;
- warhorse;
- dark horse;
- riding high.

### Key lesson

Absence is a canon fact. Missing animals, metals, gods, stars, diseases, or writing systems propagate as powerfully as presences.
