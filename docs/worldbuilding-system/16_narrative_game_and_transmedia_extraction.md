# 16. Narrative, Game, and Transmedia Extraction

This system does not exist to replace storytelling. It exists to make story, play, and adaptation grow from the world’s pressures instead of being pasted onto lore.

A canon fact becomes dramatically useful when it produces wants, fears, constraints, institutions, contradictions, and stakes.

## Extraction principle

Do not ask only:

> What story can I tell with this fact?

Ask:

> What conflicts does this fact make inevitable, profitable, sacred, illegal, humiliating, intimate, dangerous, or funny?

## Deriving conflict from facts

For a major fact, extract conflict by domain:

- **Metaphysics:** who can define what is real?
- **Physics/materiality:** who can exploit or survive the physical rule?
- **Ecology:** who adapts, invades, starves, mutates, worships, or goes extinct?
- **Geography:** who controls routes, borders, chokepoints, shelters, and exposure?
- **Technology/capability:** who can do what others cannot?
- **Magic/supernatural:** who interprets, regulates, inherits, fears, or profanes power?
- **Biology:** whose body becomes resource, stigma, weapon, or evidence?
- **Psychology:** whose desire or identity cannot survive the fact?
- **Society/culture:** whose norm becomes impossible?
- **Politics/law:** who can authorize, punish, exempt, or weaponize?
- **Economy:** who gains, loses, hoards, prices, taxes, substitutes, smuggles?
- **History:** what past injury returns?
- **Language/symbols:** what words, signs, names, songs, insults, and rituals shift?
- **Aesthetics:** what mood, genre, motif, or symbolic pattern intensifies?

## Plot engines

### Access plot

Someone needs a capability, resource, place, archive, route, license, body, ritual, or witness they cannot legally or safely access.

### Proof plot

The conflict depends on whether a claim can be proved to the relevant institution.

### Threshold plot

A fact is about to cross a scale threshold: local to regional, secret to public, rare to mass, elite to common, myth to law.

### Suppression plot

A faction tries to bury a fact, distort it, control its evidence, or punish witnesses.

### Cost-transfer plot

One group enjoys a capability while another pays the externality.

### Succession plot

A fact changes inheritance, legitimacy, memory, species continuity, dynasty, office, or apprenticeship.

### Translation plot

A fact moves between cultures, languages, species, media, institutions, or truth practices and changes meaning.

### Branch plot

Characters face incompatible continuities, versions, prophecies, campaign outcomes, or alternate histories.

### Mystery-boundary plot

The story advances by narrowing the unknown without destroying it.

## Mode-aware extraction

When extracting story, game, or medium pressure, first ask what consequence mode is load-bearing.

- In material/institutional worlds, extract conflict from access, scarcity, office, law, market, countermeasure, and ordinary-life residue.
- In mythic or fairy-tale worlds, extract conflict from prohibition, test, recurrence, helper, sacrifice, transformation, threshold, and moral reversal.
- In dream or surreal worlds, extract conflict from emotional causality, displacement, repeated images, impossible spaces, and symbolic pressure.
- In comic or absurd worlds, extract conflict from escalation, denial, disproportion, and rules followed past reason.
- In sacred or horror worlds, extract conflict from taboo, profanation, dread, contamination, forbidden knowledge, and the cost of explanation.
- In lyrical or symbolic worlds, extract conflict from motif transformation, language drift, image chains, gesture, color, sound, and excess meaning.

Extraction should not normalize every mode into the same adventure plot. A sacred opacity may generate a pilgrimage, vigil, silence, refusal, or sacrilege rather than a clue hunt.

## Game extraction

The original package warned that mechanics must not break the world. Keep that rule.

A game mechanic is a repeated permission. If a player can do something often, cheaply, and reliably, the world must show how everyone else has responded.

The MDA framework distinguishes mechanics, dynamics, and aesthetics; use that craft insight without turning the package into a formal game design spec. A mechanic is not only a rule of play; in this methodology it is also a canon pressure that creates institutions, counterplay, costs, and ordinary-life residue. See Robin Hunicke, Marc LeBlanc, and Robert Zubek, "MDA: A Formal Approach to Game Design and Game Research", https://www.cs.northwestern.edu/~hunicke/pubs/MDA.pdf.

### From canon fact to mechanic

Ask:

1. What player action does the fact permit?
2. What cost stops it from being dominant?
3. What countermeasure exists in the world?
4. Who teaches, sells, licenses, forbids, or punishes the action?
5. What non-player actors already use it?
6. What happens if players repeat it a hundred times?
7. What economies form around it?
8. What stories become impossible if it is too easy?
9. What stories become possible because it exists?

### Mechanic warning signs

- NPCs ignore a capability players use constantly.
- A cheap action should destroy travel, trade, law, combat, medicine, or secrecy but does not.
- A crafting system implies industrial capacity the world lacks.
- Resurrection exists but inheritance and murder law stay ordinary.
- Fast travel exists but borders, disease, pilgrimage, military logistics, and cities stay unchanged.
- Loot economies imply endless weapons without salvage institutions.
- Dialogue mechanics imply mind-reading social certainty but politics remain normal.

## Transmedial adaptation

Different media reveal different pressures. Adaptation branches may legitimately change canon scope.

### Prose fiction

Strengths:

- interiority;
- memory;
- unreliable narration;
- cultural texture;
- slow institutional explanation;
- controlled revelation.

Risk:

- exposition pretending to be consequence.

Prose needs ordinary-life residue and character-specific pressure more than exhaustive system explanation.

### Screen drama

Strengths:

- visible institutions;
- ritual;
- costume;
- architecture;
- gesture;
- public conflict;
- symbolic repetition.

Risk:

- invisible causal systems vanish unless externalized.

Screen adaptation should convert system consequences into scenes, props, spaces, ceremonies, queues, border checks, scars, uniforms, tools, and social behavior.

### Tabletop roleplaying

Strengths:

- player agency;
- branch canon;
- discovery;
- procedural uncertainty;
- local consequences.

Risk:

- table convenience quietly overturns world law.

A tabletop branch should name which rules of play are diegetic and which are table abstractions.

### Digital games

Strengths:

- repeated action;
- spatial exploration;
- economies;
- progression;
- systems feedback;
- player experimentation.

Risk:

- grind loops create absurd world implications.

Digital games need special scrutiny around repeatability, resource spawning, travel compression, death, inventory, dialogue certainty, and world resets.

### Comics and illustrated work

Strengths:

- visual motifs;
- symbolic juxtaposition;
- geography compressed into page design;
- artifact emphasis.

Risk:

- aesthetic coherence may override causal explanation unless the artist knows what symbols mean.

### Audio drama

Strengths:

- rumor;
- testimony;
- soundscape;
- language variation;
- institutional announcements;
- intimate uncertainty.

Risk:

- spatial and visual consequences must be made audible.

## Adaptation rule

Do not ask whether every version can be identical. Ask what must remain invariant for the world to still be itself.

Common invariants:

- core premise;
- operating law;
- central cost;
- mystery boundary;
- social wound;
- metaphysical rule;
- institutional conflict;
- aesthetic signature.

Variable elements may include timeline compression, character composites, localized geography, simplified institutions, alternate branch outcomes, or different truth-layer revelation.

## Extraction instruments

Use `templates/propagation_report.md` when story or play pressure comes from the shock cone of a major fact. Use `templates/agent_character_card.md` for characters whose wants, fears, identity, or ignorance are created by that pressure. Use `templates/action_arena_card.md` when the pressure repeats as a scene type: a trial, market, border crossing, guild test, ritual, negotiation, raid, audit, confession, or other recurring arena.

Use `templates/canon_branch_diff.md` when a medium, campaign, adaptation, or alternate outcome changes continuity rather than merely changing expression. Use `templates/aesthetic_coherence_card.md` when a medium-specific version must preserve the world’s symbolic and sensory identity.

## Extraction worksheet

For any canon fact, write:

> Because **[fact]** exists, characters can want **[desire]**, fear **[risk]**, exploit **[opportunity]**, be blocked by **[constraint]**, fight **[institution]**, uncover **[evidence]**, misunderstand **[belief]**, pay **[cost]**, and change **[world residue]**.

Then decide which medium makes that pressure most visible.


## Version 0.6 micro-examples: extracting non-naturalistic pressure

- **Mythic plot pressure:** A hero is not chosen because of destiny alone; the rite requires the hero to repeat the first theft, pay the old cost, and leave a new origin scar.
- **Absurd-comic scene engine:** Every attempt to file a complaint produces a new office whose only job is to complain about the complaint. The playable pressure is repetition-with-escalation, not random nonsense.
- **Dream-game mechanic warning:** A dream association mechanic works only if recurring images have stable triggers and costs; arbitrary surreal effects are spectacle, not extraction.

## Version 0.6 reinforcement: extraction is not canon admission

A story beat, game mechanic, scene requirement, budget solution, or medium affordance may suggest a world fact, but it does not automatically become one. Extraction asks what pressure the world can support; admission decides what canon accepts.

### Medium pressure matrix

| Medium pressure | Worldbuilding response |
|---|---|
| Novel interiority | Check belief, memory, evidence, and voice. |
| Screen compression | Check what can be shown as residue rather than exposition. |
| Tabletop improvisation | Check branch policy, unresolved debt, and canon authority at the table. |
| Game mechanics | Check repeatable capabilities, failure modes, exploit loops, and ordinary-life consequences. |
| Audio drama | Check soundscape, testimony, rumor, and unseen evidence. |
| Comic / visual serial | Check iconography, spatial staging, costume/body residue, and visual continuity. |
| Transmedia expansion | Check invariants, branch boundaries, and audience-facing mystery discipline. |

The MDA framework separates mechanics, dynamics, and aesthetics in game analysis; for this system, that means a mechanic is a repeatable permission, a dynamic is what repeated use does to the world, and an aesthetic is the experience created by those consequences.[^i4-mda]

Transmedia theory also distinguishes continuity, multiplicity, immersion, extractability, and worldbuilding; the system translates that into branch discipline and invariant anchors rather than assuming every medium must share one undifferentiated canon.[^i4-jenkins]

Klastrup and Tosca’s transmedial-world model of mythos, topos, and ethos maps cleanly to this package’s historical, spatial, and normative pressures; use it as a reminder that a world is more than a plot exported across media.[^i4-klastrup]
