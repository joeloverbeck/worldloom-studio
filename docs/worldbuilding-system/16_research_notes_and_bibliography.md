# 16. Research Notes and Bibliography

This package is practical rather than academic, but it draws on several bodies of thought.

## Systems thinking

A world is treated as an interconnected system. Facts are interventions that alter stocks, flows, feedback loops, incentives, constraints, and leverage points.

Useful concept:

- A small rule change can transform a system more than a large surface event.
- Feedback creates second- and third-order consequences.
- Delays and path dependence make worlds historically textured.

Recommended:

- Donella H. Meadows, "Leverage Points: Places to Intervene in a System."
- Donella H. Meadows, *Thinking in Systems*.

## Institutional analysis

Institutions are not just organizations. They are formal and informal rules that structure behavior.

Useful concepts:

- action arenas;
- participants;
- rules-in-use;
- formal rules versus lived practice;
- enforcement;
- incentives;
- legitimacy;
- feedback from outcomes.

Recommended:

- Elinor Ostrom, Institutional Analysis and Development framework.
- Elinor Ostrom, *Understanding Institutional Diversity*.
- Michael D. McGinnis, "An Introduction to IAD and the Language of the Ostrom Workshop."
- Daniel H. Cole, "Laws, Norms, and the Institutional Analysis and Development Framework."

## Institutional economics

Institutions shape transaction costs, production costs, uncertainty, and incentives.

Useful concepts:

- formal constraints;
- informal constraints;
- enforcement;
- transaction costs;
- path dependence;
- credible commitment;
- property rights.

Recommended:

- Douglass C. North, "Institutions."
- Douglass C. North, *Institutions, Institutional Change and Economic Performance*.

## Social-ecological systems

Worlds with resources, ecologies, magic, machines, settlements, and governance resemble social-ecological systems.

Useful concepts:

- resource systems;
- resource units;
- governance systems;
- users;
- interactions;
- outcomes;
- external social/economic/political settings;
- related ecosystems.

Recommended:

- Elinor Ostrom, "A General Framework for Analyzing Sustainability of Social-Ecological Systems."

## Storyworld and imaginary-world theory

A fictional world is more than plot background. It is a spatio-temporal totality that appears to continue beyond the story.

Useful concepts:

- storyworlds as dynamic systems;
- transmedial worlds;
- worldness;
- implied history;
- offscreen life;
- narrative access to larger reality.

Recommended:

- Mark J. P. Wolf, *Building Imaginary Worlds*.
- Marie-Laure Ryan, work on possible worlds and storyworlds.
- Lubomir Doležel, *Heterocosmica*.
- Thomas Pavel, *Fictional Worlds*.

## Knowledge representation, provenance, and belief revision

Although this package avoids software assumptions, later implementation may benefit from concepts in knowledge graphs, provenance, ontologies, and belief revision.

Useful concepts:

- entities, activities, and agents;
- provenance of claims;
- formal vocabulary and relationships;
- belief revision;
- contradiction handling;
- truth maintenance.

Recommended:

- W3C PROV-O provenance ontology.
- W3C OWL 2 overview.
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Logic of Belief Revision."
- Carlos Alchourrón, Peter Gärdenfors, and David Makinson, work on belief revision.

## Magic-system and speculative constraint craft

The package treats any repeatable world power as a capability requiring costs, limits, access, and consequences.

Useful concepts:

- hard versus soft explanatory systems;
- limitations as more important than powers;
- costs, weaknesses, and restrictions;
- mystery preservation.

Recommended:

- Brandon Sanderson, "Sanderson's First Law."
- Brandon Sanderson, "Sanderson's Second Law."

## Game design and narrative systems

For games, world facts should generate choices, tradeoffs, asymmetries, and feedback loops.

Useful concepts:

- mechanics;
- dynamics;
- aesthetics;
- player incentives;
- emergent strategy;
- faction asymmetry;
- resource loops;
- hidden information.

Recommended:

- Robin Hunicke, Marc LeBlanc, and Robert Zubek, "MDA: A Formal Approach to Game Design and Game Research."
- Katie Salen and Eric Zimmerman, *Rules of Play*.
- Tracy Fullerton, *Game Design Workshop*.

## Historical anthropology and material culture

Deep worlds are strengthened by attention to household life, material residue, ritual, food, law, class, and inherited institutions.

Recommended areas:

- economic anthropology;
- legal anthropology;
- environmental history;
- history of technology;
- archaeology of everyday life;
- historical sociology;
- military logistics;
- urban history;
- folklore studies.

## Settings worth studying

Not as models to copy, but as examples of world depth:

- *The Elder Scrolls*: local customs, metaphysical history, religious plurality, regional identity, and planar consequences.
- *Dune*: ecology, religion, economy, empire, and resource monopoly.
- Earthsea: metaphysical restraint and language-based power.
- Bas-Lag: weird ecology, institutions, and urban texture.
- Morrowind specifically: colonial law, religion, ecology, architecture, and metaphysics embedded into daily life.
- Disco Elysium: political memory, failed institutions, ideology, and place.
- Dark Souls/Elden Ring: mythic residue, broken institutions, and cosmological decay.
- Dwarf Fortress: simulation-driven material consequence.

## Research-to-system translation

This package adapts research into operational rules:

- systems thinking becomes propagation and feedback;
- institutional analysis becomes action arenas and rules-in-use;
- institutional economics becomes scarcity, transaction cost, and surplus capture;
- social-ecological systems become domain interaction;
- storyworld theory becomes offscreen life and historical residue;
- provenance becomes truth layers and source tracking;
- belief revision becomes canon admission and retcon governance;
- game design becomes extractable mechanics, choices, and feedback.
