# 01. Core Theory

## The Causal Canon premise

A fictional world is coherent when its facts behave as if they share the same reality.

This does not mean the world must be realistic. A world with gods, ghosts, psychic fungi, dream physics, cursed economics, divine bureaucracy, alien ruins, or time-traveling empires can be coherent. The requirement is stricter and more interesting:

> Once the world says a thing is possible, the rest of the world must respond.

A new fact is a disturbance moving through a system of physical constraints, resource constraints, capabilities, access bottlenecks, incentives, institutions, laws, customs, class structures, ecological pressures, religious interpretations, military adaptations, daily habits, historical memory, myths, lies, and misunderstandings.

Continuity is the floor. Causal consequence is the ceiling.

Systems thinking treats interventions as changes to stocks, flows, feedback loops, delays, incentives, and leverage points.[^meadows] This package translates that into worldbuilding craft: a fact does not merely sit in canon; it changes what the world can do next.

[^meadows]: Donella H. Meadows, "Leverage Points: Places to Intervene in a System" (1999), https://donellameadows.org/archives/leverage-points-places-to-intervene-in-a-system/

## Operating laws

### 1. Canon facts are interventions

A fact is not inert. It changes what people can do, what they fear, what they pay for, what they forbid, what they worship, what they remember, and what they lie about.

Bad worldbuilding asks:

> Can this fact fit?

Strong worldbuilding asks:

> What kind of world would have made this fact inevitable, useful, feared, regulated, exploited, forgotten, or misremembered?

### 2. No free capabilities

A capability is any repeatable ability to produce an effect.

It may be technological, magical, biological, divine, social, bureaucratic, military, economic, linguistic, ecological, or symbolic.

Examples:

- reprogramming robots;
- summoning rain;
- curing infection;
- verifying lies;
- crossing mountains cheaply;
- contacting the dead;
- preserving food;
- seeing the future;
- manufacturing gunpowder;
- binding demons with contracts;
- mobilizing an army through a kinship oath;
- causing a city to riot with a rumor.

Every capability needs prerequisites, access conditions, cost, risk, training, materials, infrastructure, maintenance, countermeasures, failure modes, institutional response, diffusion history, class implications, hostile use, and black-market pressure.

If a capability has no cost, no bottleneck, no countermeasure, and no institutional uptake, it will dominate the world.

### 3. Institutions are fossilized consequences

Institutions are recurring social solutions to recurring problems.

They include states, guilds, temples, clans, universities, courts, armies, markets, police forces, families, rituals, taboos, professional norms, criminal networks, monopolies, and informal customs.

When a capability or pressure has existed for a while, institutions should show signs of adapting to it.

If ghosts can testify, courts change. If robots can be reprogrammed, raider power changes. If teleportation is cheap, borders change. If gods speak audibly, theology and politics change. If winter lasts ten years, kinship, storage, migration, inheritance, and war change.

Institutional analysis is useful because institutions are not merely organizations; they are rules, incentives, authority, information, and enforcement arranged around recurring action situations.[^ostrom]

[^ostrom]: Ostrom Workshop, "Institutional Analysis and Development Framework", https://ostromworkshop.indiana.edu/courses-teaching/teaching-tools/iad-framework/index.html

### 4. Economics is the bloodstream of consequence

Worldbuilding often fails because it treats abilities as spectacle rather than as changes to labor, scarcity, transportation, enforcement, risk, and substitution.

Ask:

- What becomes cheaper?
- What becomes more expensive?
- What labor becomes obsolete?
- What new labor appears?
- What resources become strategic?
- What can now be stolen, taxed, insured, smuggled, licensed, rationed, or monopolized?
- What prices, rents, wages, dowries, debts, and bribes change?
- What becomes a luxury, necessity, public utility, sacred duty, or contraband?

Even a magical or mythic world has scarcity. Scarcity may be material, moral, spiritual, legal, informational, temporal, cosmic, or emotional.

### 5. Scale expands only when forced

Do not begin with a cosmology unless the world needs one.

A village can remain a village until a fact forces expansion. If a local shrine truly channels an extradimensional power, then the system must ask what dimension, what relation to other powers, what limits, why this village, who knows, what institutions surround it, and why outsiders have not already transformed it.

The world expands along causal pressure lines.

### 6. Mystery is governed, not ignored

Mystery survives when the author knows which consequences must be fixed and which explanations remain unavailable.

A mystery can remain unexplained if:

- its observable effects are consistent;
- its access conditions are controlled;
- its social interpretations vary plausibly;
- it does not conveniently solve every plot problem;
- characters respond to it according to what they believe, not according to author notes;
- apparent contradictions are assigned to truth layers rather than left unmanaged.

The author may know the explanation, partially know it, or deliberately refuse to decide. In all cases, the mystery still needs boundaries.

### 7. A world can contain contradictions without being contradictory

People lie. Records decay. Priests disagree. Empires rewrite history. Scientists mismeasure. Witnesses misremember. Myths preserve emotional truth while corrupting literal facts. Governments classify evidence. Families protect shame.

The world can contain incompatible claims. The authorial model must not contain unmanaged incompatibility.

### 8. Backstory must leave residue

If a world has experienced something important, it should be visible in ruins, law, borders, infrastructure, proverbs, cuisine, disease, festivals, jokes, architecture, kinship, trauma, inherited privilege, taboo, professional specialization, measurement systems, place names, class resentment, military doctrine, ritual calendars, family names, and bodily habits.

A past event with no residue is not history. It is trivia.

### 9. Frontloaded facts must be processed individually

A common failure: the author gives the world a rich initial brief, then asks for the world to be created all at once. The facts get included, but not propagated.

The system prevents this by treating every seed fact as if it were added later.

Initial creation is not exempt from propagation.

### 10. Coherence is not maximal explanation

The goal is not to explain everything.

The goal is to ensure that what is known, unknown, believed, hidden, disputed, obsolete, branched, and impossible all have proper places.

Over-explanation can flatten awe, comedy, horror, wonder, sacredness, and political ambiguity. Coherence does not mean all mysteries die. It means the world stops cheating.

### 11. Actors adapt under bounded knowledge

Worlds do not need omniscient optimizers. They need actors who adapt plausibly under their knowledge, identity, fear, norms, incentives, trauma, and limited capacity.

The strongest causal worlds ask both:

- What would a ruthless optimizer do?
- What would this specific actor, in this culture, with these beliefs and constraints, actually notice and attempt?

This law protects the system from turning every culture into perfect efficiency while still rejecting protagonist-only convenience.

### 12. Suppression is a consequence too

When a fact is threatening, powerful actors may bury it, distort it, monopolize it, criminalize it, mythologize it, or turn it into a test of loyalty.

Suppression must itself leave residue: censors, taboos, euphemisms, disappearances, forged records, black markets, rebels, coded songs, suspicious absences, counter-institutions, and people who profit from the silence.

## The Causal Canon cycle

Every meaningful canon change goes through this cycle:

1. State the proposed fact.
2. Reduce it to the smallest precise version.
3. Classify the fact type.
4. Assign the truth layer.
5. Assign the initial canon status.
6. Identify hard, soft, social, economic, epistemic, temporal, spatial, and aesthetic dependencies.
7. Identify capability effects and constraint composition.
8. Define access, cost, scarcity, risk, and failure.
9. Identify actors, incentives, knowledge, psychology, and identity pressures.
10. Propagate through domains.
11. Check institutions, economy, counter-institutions, and suppression politics.
12. Check geography, diffusion, and regional variation.
13. Check timeline, latency, historical residue, and path dependence.
14. Check daily life, material culture, language, and aesthetic residue.
15. Check contradictions and mystery boundaries.
16. Decide: accept, constrain, localize, historicize, reinterpret, institutionalize, price, branch, quarantine, supersede, deprecate, or reject.
17. Record the change package.
18. Schedule unresolved follow-ups.
19. Run quality assurance.
20. Protect mystery and aesthetic identity.

## Strong worldbuilding smell tests

A fact is probably under-propagated if:

- only protagonists use it;
- enemies do not optimize it;
- governments do not regulate it;
- markets do not price it;
- religions do not interpret it;
- ordinary people do not develop habits around it;
- no one has tried to counterfeit, steal, tax, suppress, teach, monopolize, ritualize, weaponize, medicalize, eroticize, domesticate, or criminalize it;
- no institution has formed around recurring use or recurring fear;
- history contains no wars, reforms, disasters, scandals, jokes, professions, proverbs, ruins, or measurements arising from it;
- it is true everywhere but has no regional variation;
- it has existed for centuries but feels newly invented;
- it changes plot outcomes without changing ordinary life;
- the world would be much easier to live in if people used the fact rationally, but they inexplicably do not.

## What the system does not require

It does not require realism.

It does not require exhaustive simulation.

It does not require every question to be answered.

It does not require a map before a story.

It does not require hard magic.

It does not require software.

It does not require an LLM.

It requires consequences.
