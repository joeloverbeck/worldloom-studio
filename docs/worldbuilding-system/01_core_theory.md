# 01. Core Theory

## The Causal Canon premise

A fictional world is coherent when its facts behave as if they share the same reality.

This does not mean the world must be realistic. A world with gods, ghosts, psychic fungi, dream physics, or time-traveling empires can be coherent. The requirement is stricter and more interesting: once the world says a thing is possible, the rest of the world must respond.

A new fact should therefore be treated as a disturbance moving through a system of:

- physical constraints;
- resource constraints;
- technological or magical capabilities;
- access bottlenecks;
- incentives;
- institutions;
- laws and customs;
- class structures;
- ecological pressures;
- religious interpretations;
- military adaptations;
- daily habits;
- historical memory;
- myths, lies, and misunderstandings.

Continuity is the floor. Causal consequence is the ceiling.

## Operating laws

### 1. Canon facts are interventions

A fact is not inert. It changes what people can do, what they fear, what they pay for, what they forbid, what they worship, and what they remember.

Bad worldbuilding asks:

> Can this fact fit?

Strong worldbuilding asks:

> What kind of world would have made this fact inevitable, useful, feared, regulated, exploited, or forgotten?

### 2. No free capabilities

A capability is any repeatable ability to produce an effect. It may be technological, magical, biological, divine, social, bureaucratic, military, or economic.

Examples:

- reprogramming robots;
- summoning rain;
- curing infection;
- verifying lies;
- crossing mountains cheaply;
- contacting the dead;
- preserving food;
- seeing the future;
- manufacturing gunpowder;
- binding demons with contracts.

Every capability needs:

- prerequisites;
- access conditions;
- cost;
- risk;
- training;
- materials;
- infrastructure;
- maintenance;
- countermeasures;
- failure modes;
- institutional response;
- diffusion history;
- class implications;
- black-market or hostile use.

If a capability has no cost, no bottleneck, no countermeasure, and no institutional uptake, it will dominate the world.

### 3. Institutions are fossilized consequences

Institutions are recurring social solutions to recurring problems. They include states, guilds, temples, clans, universities, courts, armies, markets, police forces, families, rituals, taboos, professional norms, criminal networks, monopolies, and informal customs.

When a capability or pressure has existed for a while, institutions should show signs of adapting to it.

If ghosts can testify, courts change.
If robots can be reprogrammed, raider power changes.
If teleportation is cheap, borders change.
If gods speak audibly, theology and politics change.
If winter lasts ten years, kinship, storage, migration, inheritance, and war change.

### 4. Economics is the bloodstream of consequence

Worldbuilding often fails because it treats abilities as spectacle rather than as changes to labor, scarcity, transportation, enforcement, risk, and substitution.

Ask:

- What becomes cheaper?
- What becomes more expensive?
- What labor becomes obsolete?
- What new labor appears?
- What resources become strategic?
- What can now be stolen, taxed, insured, or monopolized?
- What prices, rents, wages, dowries, debts, and bribes change?
- What becomes a luxury, necessity, public utility, or contraband?

Even a magical or mythic world has scarcity. Scarcity may be material, moral, spiritual, legal, informational, temporal, cosmic, or emotional.

### 5. Scale expands only when forced

Do not begin with a cosmology unless the world needs one.

A village can remain a village until a fact forces expansion. If a local shrine truly channels an extradimensional power, then the system must ask what dimension, what relation to other powers, what limits, why this village, who knows, and what institutions surround it.

The world expands along causal pressure lines.

### 6. Mystery is governed, not ignored

Mystery survives when the author knows which consequences must be fixed and which explanations remain unavailable.

A mystery can remain unexplained if:

- its observable effects are consistent;
- its access conditions are controlled;
- its social interpretations vary plausibly;
- it does not conveniently solve every plot problem;
- characters respond to it according to what they believe, not according to author notes;
- apparent contradictions are assigned to truth layers rather than left unmanaged.

### 7. A world can contain contradictions without being contradictory

People lie. Records decay. Priests disagree. Empires rewrite history. Scientists mismeasure. Myths preserve emotional truth while corrupting facts.

The world can contain incompatible claims. The authorial model must not contain unmanaged incompatibility.

### 8. Backstory must leave residue

If a world has experienced something important, it should be visible in:

- ruins;
- law;
- borders;
- infrastructure;
- proverbs;
- cuisine;
- disease;
- festivals;
- jokes;
- architecture;
- kinship;
- trauma;
- inherited privilege;
- taboo;
- professional specialization;
- measurement systems;
- place names;
- class resentment;
- military doctrine.

A past event with no residue is not history. It is trivia.

### 9. Frontloaded facts must be processed individually

A common failure: the author gives the world a rich initial brief, then asks for the world to be created all at once. The facts get included, but not propagated.

The system prevents this by treating every seed fact as if it were added later. Initial creation is not exempt from propagation.

### 10. Coherence is not maximal explanation

The goal is not to explain everything. The goal is to ensure that what is known, unknown, believed, hidden, and impossible all have proper places.

## The Causal Canon cycle

Every canon change goes through this cycle:

1. State the proposed fact.
2. Classify the fact.
3. Identify prerequisites.
4. Identify capability effects.
5. Identify access constraints.
6. Identify actors.
7. Predict incentives.
8. Propagate through domains.
9. Check institutions and economy.
10. Check daily life.
11. Check history.
12. Check contradictions.
13. Decide whether to accept, revise, constrain, branch, quarantine, or reject.
14. Record the change package.
15. Schedule unresolved follow-ups.
16. Protect mystery boundaries.

## Strong worldbuilding smell tests

A fact is probably under-propagated if:

- only protagonists use it;
- villains do not optimize it;
- governments do not regulate it;
- markets do not price it;
- religions do not interpret it;
- ordinary people do not develop habits around it;
- no one has tried to counterfeit, steal, tax, suppress, teach, monopolize, ritualize, weaponize, or medicalize it;
- history contains no wars, reforms, disasters, scandals, jokes, or professions arising from it;
- the world would be much easier to live in if people used the fact rationally, but they inexplicably do not.

## What the system does not require

It does not require realism.
It does not require exhaustive simulation.
It does not require every question to be answered.
It does not require a map before a story.
It does not require hard magic.
It does not require software.
It does not require an LLM.

It requires consequences.
