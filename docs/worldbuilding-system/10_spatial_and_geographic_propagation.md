# 10. Spatial and Geographic Propagation

Space is not only a map. Space is friction, access, distance, route, barrier, region, neighbor, scale, and lived place.

A fact rarely spreads everywhere evenly. Geography decides who encounters it first, who controls it, who remains outside it, and what local versions it becomes.

Spatial thinking in this document is inspired by diffusion research and geography: innovations spread through social systems over time, and nearby places often influence each other more strongly than distant ones.[^rogers][^tobler]

[^rogers]: Everett M. Rogers, Diffusion of Innovations, 5th ed.; searchable excerpt: https://eli.johogo.com/Class/DOI.pdf
[^tobler]: Waldo R. Tobler, "A Computer Movie Simulating Urban Growth in the Detroit Region", Economic Geography 46, 1970; overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobler%27s_first_law_of_geography

## The spatial questions

For any major fact, ask:

1. Where is it true?
2. Where is it known?
3. Where is it usable?
4. Where is it legal, sacred, taboo, or impossible?
5. What routes carry it?
6. What barriers block it?
7. What centers control it?
8. What peripheries distort it?
9. What regional residues prove it has moved through space?

## Spatial scopes

A fact may be:

- room-bound;
- household-bound;
- settlement-bound;
- institution-bound;
- route-bound;
- ecological-zone-bound;
- region-bound;
- borderland-bound;
- empire-bound;
- species-range-bound;
- planar or cosmological;
- dream-bound;
- everywhere in principle but locally constrained in practice.

State scope before propagation.

## Friction

Friction is anything that slows, distorts, or blocks movement.

Spatial friction includes:

- distance;
- mountains;
- rivers;
- seas;
- deserts;
- climate;
- monsters;
- disease;
- border law;
- tolls;
- piracy;
- language;
- class exclusion;
- sacred prohibition;
- terrain unsuitable for infrastructure;
- incompatible ecology;
- information delay;
- institutional monopoly;
- fear.

Friction creates regional variation.

## Corridors

Corridors accelerate movement.

Examples:

- rivers;
- roads;
- pilgrim routes;
- trade caravans;
- rail lines;
- portals;
- ley lines;
- migration paths;
- military roads;
- rumor networks;
- shipping lanes;
- kinship chains;
- refugee routes;
- schools;
- fairs;
- ports;
- battlefronts.

If a fact diffuses, identify its corridors.

## Barriers

Barriers block or transform movement.

Examples:

- geography;
- law;
- climate;
- taboo;
- monopoly;
- species biology;
- language;
- disease quarantine;
- ritual purity;
- political border;
- secret order;
- lack of maintenance;
- cost;
- mythic boundary.

A barrier should leave residue: forts, tolls, smugglers, border dialects, quarantine bells, forbidden maps, mountain shrines, dead roads, specialized guides, or local legends.

## Centers and peripheries

Centers often receive, standardize, tax, teach, and monopolize new facts.

Peripheries often preserve older forms, develop workarounds, become testing grounds, resist control, or turn central rules into local practice.

Ask:

- What city, temple, archive, port, gate, mine, court, school, or ruin is the central place for this fact?
- What places depend on that center?
- What places reject or reinterpret it?
- What borderlands become rich, violent, sacred, or strange because of it?

## Settlement pressure

A fact can change where people live.

It may create:

- gate towns;
- fortress villages;
- quarantine islands;
- shrine economies;
- mining camps;
- river cities;
- roadless sanctuaries;
- floating markets;
- border bureaucracies;
- dead zones;
- seasonal camps;
- nomad routes;
- underground cities;
- ruin scavenger camps.

If geography remains unchanged after a geography-changing fact, explain why.

## Infrastructure pressure

Repeated movement creates infrastructure.

Look for:

- roads;
- bridges;
- canals;
- ports;
- customs houses;
- waystations;
- signal towers;
- gate plazas;
- quarantine yards;
- border walls;
- warehouses;
- waterworks;
- shrine paths;
- repair depots;
- warning markers;
- maps.

Infrastructure is fossilized travel demand.

## Regional variation

A world gains depth when different regions answer the same pressure differently.

For each region, ask:

- What local resource changes the fact?
- What local institution controls it?
- What local belief interprets it?
- What local constraint limits it?
- What neighbor pressures it?
- What local history distorts it?
- What ordinary habit is unique here?

Avoid making every city the same culture with different names.

## Spatial diffusion patterns

### Contagious diffusion

The fact spreads through contact from neighbor to neighbor.

Use for disease, rumors, fashions, rituals, language features, folk practices, and local technologies.

### Hierarchical diffusion

The fact spreads from major centers to smaller centers.

Use for elite fashion, law, bureaucracy, military doctrine, university knowledge, and imperial religion.

### Relocation diffusion

The fact moves with migrants, refugees, missionaries, soldiers, merchants, colonists, exiles, or captives.

Use for diasporic customs, transplanted crops, secret arts, and plague carriers.

### Network diffusion

The fact spreads along non-geographic links: guilds, kinship, correspondence, dreams, telepathy, pilgrimage vows, or covert cells.

Use when distance matters less than membership.

### Suppressed diffusion

The fact would spread, but power blocks it.

Use for censored knowledge, illegal capabilities, quarantined diseases, forbidden languages, and monopolized resources.

Suppression creates leaks.

## Spatial mismatch

A spatial mismatch occurs when canon ignores geography.

Examples:

- desert cities eat as if surrounded by fertile farmland;
- mountain villages have cheap ocean fish;
- news crosses empires faster than travel allows;
- a plague ignores trade routes;
- every region has identical law despite weak central authority;
- a monster-infested forest has no impact on roads, settlement, or children’s habits.

Repair by adding routes, local production, elite import, seasonal scarcity, magic logistics, regional variation, or acknowledging contradiction.

## Map discipline

A map is useful when it reveals pressure.

A map should answer:

- where travel is easy;
- where travel is dangerous;
- where resources are;
- where institutions can project power;
- where culture diffuses;
- where geography preserves difference;
- where conflict becomes inevitable.

Do not draw map detail that implies logistics you are unwilling to honor.

## Spatial audit

For every major fact:

- [ ] Spatial scope is defined.
- [ ] Corridors are identified.
- [ ] Barriers are identified.
- [ ] Centers and peripheries respond differently.
- [ ] Infrastructure matches repeated pressure.
- [ ] Regional variation exists where geography demands it.
- [ ] Diffusion speed is plausible.
- [ ] Local beliefs and institutions reflect place.
