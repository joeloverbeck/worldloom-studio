# 09. Temporal and Timeline Protocol

Time is not a list of dates. Time is causality under sequence, duration, delay, memory, and residue.

A world can survive vague calendars. It cannot survive events that happen in the wrong causal order.

## The temporal questions

For any major fact, ask:

1. When did it become true?
2. When did someone first notice?
3. When did it become teachable, repeatable, profitable, feared, or regulated?
4. How long would adaptation take?
5. What happened before institutions understood it?
6. What residue should remain after years, generations, or centuries?
7. What is too early, too late, or suspiciously absent?

## Date types

### Event date

When something happened.

### Discovery date

When someone learned it happened or learned how it works.

### Public date

When a defined public became aware.

### Institutional date

When institutions reacted.

### Ordinary-life date

When habits, prices, tools, jokes, or household routines changed.

### Mythic date

When a community began remembering or ritualizing the event, whether accurately or not.

### Authorial revision date

When the authorial model changed. This may not correspond to any in-world date.

Do not confuse these. A fact can be objectively ancient, publicly recent, institutionally suppressed, and mythically misdated.

## Timeline granularity

Use the lightest granularity that preserves causal order.

Options:

- before / after;
- same season;
- same reign;
- generation;
- era;
- exact year;
- exact day;
- ritual cycle;
- campaign session;
- draft phase;
- mythic age;
- subjective time.

Exact dates are useful when logistics, inheritance, war, travel, aging, evidence, legal deadlines, or retcons depend on them.

## Latency

Latency is the delay between a fact becoming true and the world adapting.

Common delays:

- discovery delay;
- proof delay;
- travel delay;
- training delay;
- manufacturing delay;
- adoption delay;
- legal delay;
- generational delay;
- taboo delay;
- suppression delay;
- ecological delay;
- aesthetic reveal delay.

Latency explains why a powerful fact has not yet transformed everything.

It must be plausible.

## Era design

An era is not a costume palette. It is a stable pattern of constraints.

Define an era by:

- dominant capabilities;
- dominant constraints;
- major institutions;
- economic basis;
- transport and communication speed;
- knowledge system;
- religious or mythic order;
- ordinary-life routines;
- unresolved contradictions;
- aesthetic promise.

An era changes when enough of these shift.

## The “first true” test

For any fact, ask:

> When was this first true?

Then ask:

> If it was true then, what should already have changed by now?

If nothing changed, the fact may be:

- too recent;
- too local;
- too secret;
- too costly;
- too unreliable;
- suppressed;
- misunderstood;
- false;
- branch-only;
- or under-propagated.

## Historical residue by timescale

### Days to months

Look for rumors, panic, emergency rules, opportunistic crime, early adopters, denial, scapegoats, first accidents, temporary rituals, and price spikes.

### Years

Look for licenses, specialist labor, lawsuits, school changes, black markets, insurance, local architecture, military experiments, songs, jokes, and new etiquette.

### Generations

Look for inherited professions, family strategies, institutional legitimacy, class divides, inherited trauma, migration, myth, normalized tools, old reforms, and regional variation.

### Centuries

Look for ruins, borders, dead laws, sacred anniversaries, fossilized bureaucracy, extinct professions, altered language, city placement, calendars, and civilizations shaped around the fact.

## Sequence integrity

A timeline breaks when effects precede causes without explanation.

Common sequence failures:

- an institution predates the pressure that created it;
- a law responds to a capability not yet discovered;
- a culture has centuries of taboo around a recent event;
- a war uses logistics that do not exist yet;
- a character remembers an event from before their birth without a mechanism;
- a technology spreads faster than communication allows;
- a retcon inserts a fact that should have altered later canon.

Repair by historicizing, localizing, adding precursor events, reframing the institution, branching, or making the apparent sequence error diegetic misinformation.

## Retrospective insertion

When adding a fact to an existing world, treat it as a timeline disturbance.

Ask:

- Has this always been true?
- If yes, what old scenes, institutions, maps, prices, beliefs, or conflicts should already reflect it?
- If not, when did it become true?
- Who noticed first?
- What changed before the main story reached it?
- Is reader/player-facing explanation needed?

Retrospective insertion is safest when the fact is local, secret, constrained, misunderstood, or historically latent.

## Timeline branches

A branch needs:

- divergence point;
- changed fact;
- unchanged baseline facts;
- consequences unique to the branch;
- conflicts with baseline;
- merge possibility or no-merge declaration;
- affected characters, institutions, and mysteries.

Use `templates/canon_branch_diff.md`.

## Temporal mystery

Time can be mysterious without being unmanaged.

Allowed temporal mysteries:

- cyclical history;
- prophecy that may be self-fulfilling;
- missing years;
- records that disagree;
- gods outside time;
- unreliable calendars;
- memories from alternate branches;
- effects without known origins.

Still record observable boundaries and forbidden uses.

## Timeline audit

Run `checklists/temporal_timeline_sweep.md` for any Level 2 or higher fact with first appearance, discovery, public knowledge, institutional reaction, branch divergence, retcon, prophecy, inheritance, war, migration, law, aging, or evidence implications. Use `templates/temporal_timeline_card.md` when the timing itself becomes load-bearing.

For every major fact:

- [ ] It has a first-true date or relative sequence.
- [ ] It has a first-known date if knowledge matters.
- [ ] Institutional reaction time is plausible.
- [ ] Diffusion speed is plausible.
- [ ] Historical residue matches age.
- [ ] Existing canon does not ignore the fact retroactively.
- [ ] Branches have divergence points.
- [ ] Mysteries have temporal boundaries.

## Version 0.4 reinforcement: latency ladder

Use a latency ladder to keep history from feeling instantaneous:

| Latency band | Typical residues |
|---|---|
| Immediate | panic, injury, first use, first rumor, first failure. |
| Days/weeks | emergency rules, opportunists, local improvisation, witness distortion. |
| Months/years | licensing, training, countermeasures, prices, migrations, professions. |
| Generations | inheritance, class structure, myths, architecture, calendars, family names. |
| Eras | ruins, borders, sacred history, obsolete law, fossilized metaphors, old resentments. |

A fact that is old but has only immediate residues has failed the temporal test.

### Chronology pluralism

The world may contain multiple chronologies:

- official chronology;
- local memory;
- ritual calendar;
- academic reconstruction;
- suppressed archive;
- branch chronology;
- mythic time;
- personal trauma time.

Do not collapse these into one timeline unless the world’s institutions have the power and motive to enforce a single calendar.

### Retcon temporal rule

A retcon must state whether it changes:

- what objectively happened;
- what was known;
- what was recorded;
- what was believed;
- what was hidden;
- what branch applies;
- what the audience was allowed to infer.
