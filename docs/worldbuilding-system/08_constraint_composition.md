# 08. Constraint Composition

Constraints are not decorative limits. They are causal machinery.

A constraint explains why a powerful fact does not flatten the world. It also creates new behaviors: loopholes, professions, rituals, markets, resentment, crimes, expertise, and failure modes.

## What counts as a constraint

A constraint is any limit on a fact, capability, process, actor, institution, or claim.

Common forms:

- physical;
- biological;
- magical;
- divine;
- legal;
- social;
- economic;
- informational;
- psychological;
- ecological;
- temporal;
- geographic;
- moral;
- aesthetic;
- epistemic.

A constraint may limit:

- who can use a capability;
- when it works;
- where it works;
- how often it works;
- what it costs;
- what it risks;
- how it fails;
- what it affects;
- what evidence reveals it;
- who believes it;
- whether it fits the world’s tone.

## The constraint test

A constraint is strong when it answers all five questions:

1. What exactly does it prevent?
2. What does it still allow?
3. Who knows the boundary?
4. Who tries to cross it?
5. What residue exists because the boundary matters?

Weak constraint:

> Teleportation is hard.

Strong constraint:

> Teleportation requires paired gates built from river-stone quarried only during eclipse tides. It cannot carry iron, infected blood, or unwilling passengers. Every jump leaves a public arrival trace at both gates. Gate-priests license crossings, smugglers use bone substitutes, and border cities have arrival courts.

## Composition types

### Stacked constraints

Multiple limits apply at once.

Example: only trained healers can perform the cure, only at temples, only during fever moons, and only if the patient survives the ritual fast.

Stacking narrows access but increases institutional pressure. Every layer needs keepers, evaders, and resentment.

### Gate constraints

One condition unlocks or blocks all downstream use.

Example: no spell works unless the caster knows the true name.

Gate constraints create secrecy, credentialing, name theft, memory protection, and identity rituals.

### Tradeoff constraints

A use is possible only by paying one cost against another.

Example: a resurrection restores the body but removes a year of memory from a loved one.

Tradeoffs create moral economy, family conflict, law, taboo, fraud, and sacrificial professions.

### Threshold constraints

A capability works only above or below a threshold.

Example: the shield protects villages under 500 people but fails around cities.

Thresholds create settlement patterns, census fraud, legal definitions, splinter villages, and contested counting.

### Sequential constraints

A fact works only after steps occur in order.

Example: a dead witness must be bound, named, washed, questioned, released, and archived before dawn.

Sequential constraints create procedures, specialists, bottlenecks, and failure points.

### Environmental constraints

A capability depends on weather, terrain, ecology, season, celestial condition, architecture, or metaphysical zone.

Environmental constraints create regional variation and pilgrimage geography.

### Social-permission constraints

A capability works or is tolerated only when a person has status, oath, license, ritual purity, class standing, consent, or community sanction.

Social-permission constraints create gatekeepers, corruption, initiation, exclusion, and rebellion.

### Knowledge constraints

A fact depends on knowing, remembering, proving, interpreting, or concealing something.

Knowledge constraints create schools, archives, censors, mnemonics, propaganda, and dangerous witnesses.

### Aesthetic constraints

A fact is allowed only in forms that preserve the world’s emotional identity.

A horror world may allow healing only at costs that deepen dread. A mythic world may require beauty, oath, or fate. A satire may let rules become absurd but not emotionally random.

### Preservation constraints

A preservation constraint limits explanation, use, or adaptation in order to protect wonder, sacred opacity, horror, dread, or symbolic excess. It does not say “anything goes.” It says which interpretive moves would damage the effect.

Examples:

- The saint's well may heal, but only those who do not ask whether the water is alive may drink.
- The black door may recur in dreams, but mapping its hinges turns the dream into a dead hallway.
- The monster's origin may remain forbidden, but its contamination rules must stay consistent.

A preservation constraint should name the protected effect, the prohibited explanation or use, and the consequences that remain observable.

## Constraint conflict

Constraints can contradict each other.

Examples:

- A spell requires public chanting but must remain secret.
- A cure is illegal but the state relies on it in war.
- A gate works only with consent, but prisoners are routinely transported through it.
- A sacred taboo forbids corpse disturbance, but courts require dead testimony.

When constraints conflict, decide whether the conflict is:

- hypocrisy;
- corruption;
- rare exception;
- institutional compromise;
- legal fiction;
- regional difference;
- historical residue;
- genuine contradiction requiring repair.

## Constraint leakage

No important constraint is perfect unless perfection is itself a major world fact.

Ask:

- How do people test the boundary?
- What loopholes exist?
- What partial workarounds exist?
- What rumors exaggerate the loophole?
- Who profits from selling false bypasses?
- What accidents reveal the true limit?
- What countermeasure patches the leak?

Leakage is often more story-rich than the constraint itself.

## Constraint chains

One constraint creates another.

Example:

1. Robot reprogramming requires diagnostic stations.
2. Stations require power.
3. Power requires rare cells.
4. Cells require trade with dangerous ruins.
5. Dangerous ruins require escorts.
6. Escorts require payment.
7. Payment creates raider taxes and mechanic ransom.

The chain turns a technical limit into economy, violence, politics, and ordinary fear.

## Constraint residues

A real constraint leaves residue.

Look for:

- warning signs;
- rituals;
- procedures;
- licenses;
- professions;
- jokes;
- training scars;
- black markets;
- counterfeit tools;
- legal definitions;
- insurance exclusions;
- architecture;
- measuring devices;
- etiquette;
- myths explaining the limit;
- children’s rules.

## Constraint abuse

Institutions use constraints to create power.

A real limit can become an excuse for monopoly. A false limit can become a tool of class control. A sacred limit can hide economic interest. A technical limit can be exaggerated to preserve professional status.

Ask:

- Who benefits from the constraint being believed?
- Who benefits from the constraint being misunderstood?
- Who knows the constraint is weaker than advertised?
- Who is punished for testing it?

## Constraint repair

When a fact breaks the world, prefer constraints that produce more world rather than less.

Bad repair:

> Teleportation exists but nobody uses it.

Better repair:

> Teleportation exists through licensed gates that cannot carry grain, iron, corpses, or contagious breath; every crossing leaves a taxable trace; border cults control the gates; smugglers specialize in exempt goods; armies fight over arrival plazas.

The repair does not merely block consequences. It redirects them into institutions, markets, and daily life.

For any major constraint:

- [ ] It states what is limited.
- [ ] It states what remains possible.
- [ ] It has a cause or accepted mystery boundary.
- [ ] It has access implications.
- [ ] It creates cost, risk, or delay.
- [ ] It has countermeasures or loopholes unless perfection is justified.
- [ ] It leaves daily-life residue.
- [ ] It leaves institutional or economic residue.
- [ ] It does not exist only to protect the plot.


## Version 0.6 micro-examples: constraints in non-naturalistic modes

- **Ritual gate constraint:** A river answers only questions asked barefoot. The constraint is not “soft mood”; it limits access, shapes pilgrimage behavior, creates seasonal risk, and produces counterfeit barefoot rites.
- **Dream tradeoff constraint:** A key opens any door but replaces one remembered face with the door's color. The cost composes with memory, identity, evidence, and character agency rather than with money.
- **Sacred preservation constraint:** The saint's name cannot be spoken inside the chapel. This may protect opacity, but it cannot protect contradictions: records, gestures, substitutes, and consequences of violation still need governance.

## Version 0.6 reinforcement: constraint budgets

A constraint is strong when it creates story pressure while preventing domination. It is weak when it exists only to excuse why a world-breaking fact does not matter.

Use a **constraint budget** for every major capability:

- one access constraint;
- one cost constraint;
- one failure or risk constraint;
- one social or institutional constraint;
- one countermeasure or loophole.

More constraints may be needed, but too many unrelated constraints make the fact feel patched rather than designed.

### Constraint stress test

Ask five hostile questions:

1. What does a rich actor do to bypass this?
2. What does a desperate actor do to bypass this?
3. What does a state, temple, guild, army, corporation, clan, or criminal network do to monopolize this?
4. What does a child, servant, dissident, exile, disabled person, prisoner, or nonhuman actor discover that elites miss?
5. What loophole becomes common enough to create a profession, taboo, punishment, black market, or myth?

### Constraint residues

A real constraint leaves residue. It changes tools, warnings, pricing, ceremonies, training, architecture, slang, jokes, scars, insurance, law, or superstition. If a constraint leaves no residue, it may be authorial duct tape.
