# 11. Agent and Character Psychology

World facts become stories when they collide with particular people.

The system must handle institutions, economies, and domains, but worlds are lived by actors with identity, fear, habits, status, memory, norms, trauma, ambition, and limited knowledge.

This document is not a psychological theory manual. It is a craft protocol for preventing characters from becoming plot puppets or perfect optimizers.

Ideas here draw lightly from bounded rationality, planned behavior, and social identity: people act under limited information, perceived control, norms, attitudes, and group membership.[^simon][^ajzen][^tajfel]

## Actor pressure model

For any actor affected by a fact, track:

- what they know;
- what they believe;
- what they want;
- what they fear;
- what they can actually do;
- what their group expects;
- what they cannot admit;
- what they misperceive;
- what habit they fall back on;
- what cost they cannot afford;
- what identity they protect;
- what memory or trauma narrows their choices.

A plausible actor response emerges where capability, belief, incentive, and identity meet.

## The five actor gates

A world fact changes an actor only if it passes one or more gates.

### 1. Awareness

Does the actor know the fact exists?

If not, what rumor, evidence, witness, institution, or accident would reveal it?

### 2. Interpretation

What does the actor think it means?

A priest, smuggler, child, scientist, soldier, widow, and landlord may interpret the same fact differently.

### 3. Capacity

Can the actor do anything about it?

Capacity includes skill, money, status, time, body, tools, legal permission, allies, courage, and perceived control.

### 4. Incentive

Does the actor have reason to act?

Incentive may be survival, profit, shame, faith, love, revenge, curiosity, duty, prestige, fear, hunger, or belonging.

### 5. Permission

Does the actor’s social world allow action?

Permission includes law, taboo, family expectation, class role, gender role, caste, oath, profession, religion, and genre identity.

## Character roles generated by world facts

Major facts naturally create character roles.

### Keeper

Controls access.

Examples: gate priest, archive monk, firmware technician, mortuary advocate, court censor.

### Excluded person

Needs access but is denied.

Examples: poor heir unable to bind a dead witness, village outside the teleport network, immigrant whose dialect is treated as criminal.

### Loophole finder

Discovers practical cracks between rule and use.

Examples: smuggler, folk healer, apprentice, corrupt clerk, child who does not know the taboo.

### Believer

Acts from mythic, public, or local belief whether or not objective canon agrees.

### Skeptic

Rejects the dominant interpretation, rightly or wrongly.

### Victim of residue

Inherits cost from a past fact.

Examples: descendants of gate plague survivors, families stigmatized by old robot worship, regions taxed for ancient rebellion.

### Converter

Translates a fact between domains.

Examples: scientist makes ritual measurable, merchant turns taboo into market, soldier turns cure into weapon, poet turns disaster into identity.

### Suppressed witness

Knows something power wants buried.

### Aesthetic carrier

Embodies the world’s tone through habit, speech, dress, ritual, or contradiction.

## Psychological residue

Facts leave marks inside people.

Look for:

- phobias;
- superstitions;
- expertise;
- denial;
- learned helplessness;
- professional pride;
- religious awe;
- resentment;
- gallows humor;
- family scripts;
- class aspiration;
- shame;
- trauma;
- ritualized caution;
- fatalism;
- obsession;
- identity politics;
- nostalgia for the pre-fact world.

If teleportation exists, some people fear arrival chambers. If the dead testify, some people plan their last words. If robots can be hacked, children learn which machines sleep lightly.

## The bounded-optimization rule

Always run two passes:

1. **Hostile optimization** — what would a competent enemy do if they were ruthless, informed, and resourced?
2. **Situated agency** — what would this actor actually do with their knowledge, beliefs, resources, identity, and constraints?

The first pass prevents convenience.

The second prevents everyone from becoming the same hyper-rational strategist.

## Belief and dissonance

When facts contradict identity, actors may not update cleanly.

They may:

- deny evidence;
- reinterpret the fact;
- blame outsiders;
- double down on ritual;
- split public compliance from private belief;
- follow law while mocking it;
- create conspiracy theories;
- convert dramatically;
- collapse into shame;
- weaponize ambiguity.

Do not use psychology to excuse incoherence. Use it to make different responses plausible.

## Social identity

Actors belong to groups.

Group membership shapes:

- what evidence is trusted;
- which institutions feel legitimate;
- who can marry whom;
- what work is honorable;
- what speech is proper;
- what risk is heroic versus shameful;
- what knowledge is betrayal;
- what violence is justified.

A fact that changes group boundaries changes identity.

Examples:

- Ghost testimony creates families who become “witness-rich” or “witness-poor.”
- Robot maintenance creates a mechanic caste, machine cult, or stigmatized profession.
- Teleport gates create gate-born elites and road-born outsiders.

## Character contradiction versus canon contradiction

Characters may be inconsistent.

A person can believe one thing, say another, do a third, and rationalize the fourth.

This is not a canon contradiction if:

- the inconsistency is visible;
- it has motive or pressure;
- it affects consequences;
- the authorial model knows the difference.

It becomes bad writing when inconsistency only serves scene convenience.

## Agent-level propagation questions

For each major fact, choose at least three actor positions:

- beneficiary;
- loser;
- gatekeeper;
- excluded person;
- enforcer;
- believer;
- skeptic;
- fraud;
- victim;
- child;
- elder;
- criminal;
- reformer;
- collaborator;
- rebel.

For each, answer:

1. What changes in their daily life?
2. What do they want now?
3. What are they wrong about?
4. What habit or norm guides them?
5. What would they risk?
6. What would they never risk?
7. What story pressure do they generate?

## Character card use

Use `templates/agent_character_card.md` for characters whose psychology, role, or identity carries world pressure.

Do not fill a card for every person. Use it where world facts and character stakes intersect.

## Agent audit

Run `checklists/agent_character_sweep.md` when a fact changes what a character or intentional actor can know, want, fear, afford, misread, or ethically tolerate. Use `templates/agent_character_card.md` only for actors whose psychology carries world pressure; do not card every person.

A major fact is weak if:

- no ordinary person changes behavior;
- no actor misunderstands it;
- no one is excluded by it;
- every actor responds with the same logic;
- enemies ignore it;
- institutions adapt but characters do not;
- characters change but institutions do not;
- trauma, memory, identity, or belief would matter but remain absent.


## Version 0.6 micro-examples: agency under non-naturalistic pressure

- **Fairy-tale agency:** A youngest child who feeds the fox is not “irrational”; within the mode, generosity to a helper is actor-local rationality because helper logic recurs.
- **Dream agency:** A character avoids a blue staircase because blue has repeatedly condensed guilt, not because the staircase is materially dangerous.
- **Sacred/horror agency:** Refusing to photograph the relic may be rational when prior profanations produced costs, even if the origin of the relic is protected from explanation.

## Version 0.6 reinforcement: bounded agency is not stupidity

Bounded rationality means actors face limits in information, alternatives, foresight, computation, time, status, and emotional capacity; it does not mean they are foolish.[^i4-simon] Strong worlds give actors local intelligence. They adapt plausibly from where they stand.

### Actor-local rationality

Before calling an actor irrational, ask:

1. What do they know?
2. What do they think they know?
3. What are they forbidden or ashamed to know?
4. What do they value more than survival, profit, efficiency, or truth?
5. What action would be rational inside their identity, trauma, role, class, religion, oath, family, species, or institution?

### Character pressure from world facts

A world fact becomes character pressure when it changes:

- what the actor can safely desire;
- what they can admit knowing;
- what they owe;
- what they fear becoming;
- what they can prove;
- what they can inherit;
- what community they can remain part of;
- what betrayal would cost.

### Anti-optimization rule

For every major capability, identify both:

- the ruthless optimizer’s move;
- the culturally plausible move most actors actually make.

The gap between them is often where story lives.

## Agency in non-naturalistic modes

Mode-awareness does not erase actors. In mythic, fairy-tale, dream, comic, sacred, or horror worlds, agency may be bounded by oath, role, taboo, archetype, temptation, recurrent test, ritual speech, prophetic name, dread, or symbolic transformation rather than by ordinary institutional incentives alone.

Ask:

- What does the mode make thinkable or unthinkable for this actor?
- What recurrent role pressures the actor: youngest child, trickster, witness, sacrificer, double, fool, guardian, taboo-breaker?
- What transformation would reveal a hidden desire, guilt, oath, shame, blessing, or contamination?
- What does the actor refuse to know because knowing would profane, doom, or unmake them?

Do not use archetype as a substitute for motive. Use it as an additional pressure on motive.
